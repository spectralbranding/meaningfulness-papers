## Abstract

This paper empirically demonstrates Zharnikov's (2026ao) Proposition P4 — rendering-equivalence under spine-preservation — in management theory. The paper extends the companion theory's Heisenberg–Schrödinger historical existence proof into contemporary strategy research via structural extractions of two independently-authored pairs: a dynamic-capabilities pair (Eisenhardt and Martin 2000 + Zollo and Winter 2002) and a knowledge-based-view pair from the SMJ Winter 1996 Special Issue (Grant 1996 + Liebeskind 1996). The recombination metric Rec returns 4 linked propositions with preserved antecedents on each pair. A random-graph null baseline shows Pr(Rec ≥ 3 by chance) ≈ .000 across 1,000 size-matched shadows. Three additional renderings of substrates already in the corpus — a practitioner-register rendering of the paper's own structure, a third rendering of the focal-pair shared substrate, and a cross-paper rendering of the companion theory's full theoretical apparatus — preserve 11/14, 4/4, and 12/15 items strictly; 14/14, 4/4, and 15/15 semantically; zero contradictions. A bibliographic-hallucination audit of twelve AI-suggested anchors finds two verified and ten negative findings. Secondary β/δ estimates satisfy the cost-asymmetry ordering. Cross-language and inter-coder reliability tests are pre-registered for the next release. The paper engages recombinant-search and knowledge-representation scholarship as theoretical antecedents.

**Keywords**: generative AI, rendering-equivalence, spine-preservation, structural substrate, dynamic capabilities, knowledge-based view, working-paper format, organizational knowledge work.

---

Generative AI shifts the cost structure of organizational knowledge work asymmetrically across the layers of the artifacts knowledge workers produce. Zharnikov (2026ao) introduces the Operator role as a role-level abstraction above the human-versus-AI instance distinction and decomposes any knowledge artifact into three layers: the log substrate L of authoring events, the semantic substrate or spine S extracted from L, and the cohort-conditional prose rendering R produced from S. The companion theory states P1 (separability of substrate from rendering) and P4 (rendering-equivalence under spine-preservation) as primary propositions of the theory paper. P4 specifically holds that two prose renderings of a locked substrate converge on conclusions if and only if both renderings preserve the substrate's structural elements, under axiom A1 (σ-faithfulness on locked subsets). The companion theory illustrates P4 in Online Appendix D on the founding papers of quantum mechanics — Heisenberg's matrix mechanics (1925) and Schrödinger's wave mechanics (1926), later shown by Schrödinger (1926, equivalence paper) and von Neumann (1932) to be mathematically equivalent renderings of the same underlying spine — as a historical existence proof at extreme formalism distance.

This paper extends the P4 demonstration from physics into contemporary management research. The central contribution is empirical evidence that two independently-authored management-theory papers, treated as two prose renderings of a shared underlying substrate, preserve the substrate's structural elements at the typed-DAG level Zharnikov (2026ao) Online Appendix A specifies. We report retroactive spine extractions of two such pairs. The focal pair is Eisenhardt and Martin (2000) *Strategic Management Journal* and Zollo and Winter (2002) *Organization Science* on the formation and evolution of dynamic capabilities. The knowledge-based-view (KBV) pair is Grant (1996) and Liebeskind (1996) in the same *Strategic Management Journal* Winter Special Issue "Knowledge and the Firm." On each pair, the recombination metric Rec(G₁, G₂) — defined as the size of the maximum common subgraph that preserves both node typing and antecedent edges — returns Rec = 4 on linked propositions with preserved antecedents. The Rec values do not measure organizational recombination rates (a separate proposition, P2 in the companion theory, deferred to large-N work). The Rec values measure structural-substrate preservation across two prose renderings: direct evidence for P4 at the contemporary management-research artifact level.

The substantive contribution is twofold. *First*, this paper supplies the first rigorous structural test of rendering-equivalence in management theory — the typed-DAG isomorphism analysis on the focal pair and the KBV pair, with random-shadow null at the 99th+ percentile, demonstrates that the P4 preservation result Zharnikov (2026ao) Online Appendix D establishes on the Heisenberg–Schrödinger founding pair in physics also holds on contemporary management-research artifacts. The test is *structural* (typed-DAG node typing + antecedent-edge preservation) rather than lexical (vocabulary overlap) or topical (subject-matter coincidence), and the null baseline rules out chance attribution under a 1,000-shadow random-graph comparison. *Second*, the empirical demonstration carries operational implications for post-AI scholarly and organizational knowledge production: the structural substrate of a knowledge artifact is a first-class object distinct from its prose rendering; the substrate is comparable across renderings, reproducible at the typed-DAG level, and amenable to AI verification at the sub-linear cost the companion theory's β specification names. Grimes, von Krogh, Feuerriegel, Rink, and Gruber (2023) describe the field-level shift from scarcity to abundance in scholarship that this paper's empirical findings operationalize at the artifact level.

Two secondary contributions accompany the central P4 demonstration. First, grid-anchored illustrative estimates of the cost-function exponents β (AI-projection substrate verification cost) and δ (human-projection rendering verification cost) across five available substrate spines and five renderings satisfy the companion theory's β < 1 < δ ordering. These are reported as supportive evidence for P3 (verification-cost asymmetry), a separate companion-theory proposition whose organizational-outcome-scale empirical test lives at v4.0.0 of this working-paper trajectory; this release does not claim to test P3 at recombination-rate scale. Second, a pre-registered iterative-cohort-growth protocol with Zenodo-version-DOI discipline turns the working-paper trajectory v1.0.0 → v4.0.0 into a longitudinal self-test of P4 — each release of this paper is itself a rendering of a growing structural substrate, and the trajectory across releases reads as a self-application of P4 to the paper's own production.

Three additional pieces of evidence land at this release. A random-graph null-baseline computation shows that the observed Rec = 4 on each twin pair sits above the 99th percentile of a 1,000-shadow random-shadow distribution at the same node count and node-type distribution — the Rec values are not attributable to chance under the random null. A practitioner-register Substack rendering of the present paper's own substrate (1,615 words; different audience, prose conventions, length, tone) preserves 11 of 14 locked propositions at the strict typed-DAG level and 14 of 14 at the semantic level with zero contradictions, under same-operator re-extraction. A LinkedIn rendering of the focal-pair shared substrate (1,044 words; arrow-bullet structure; named antecedents in plain prose) preserves all four substrate items and their antecedent edges. Together these three pieces of evidence extend the P4 evidence base in this release from two twin pairs at small N to two twin pairs plus a random-shadow null comparison plus two self-application renderings of substrates already in the corpus.

The remainder of the paper unfolds as follows. The Theory section places the paper's framework in conversation with adjacent literatures on recombinant search (Kaplan and Vakili 2015), boundary objects (Carlile 2004), automation–augmentation in management (Raisch and Krakowski 2021), resource origins (Felin, Kauffman, and Zenger 2023), and organizational learning (Levinthal and March 1993; Levitt and March 1988), before stating the elements of Zharnikov (2026ao) that Paper B directly extends. The Method section specifies the retroactive spine-extraction procedure, the typed-DAG maximum-common-subgraph recombination metric, the random-graph null baseline, and the cost-function calibration procedure. The Results section reports the Rec values on both twin pairs as the central P4 demonstration, the null-baseline comparison, the supportive β/δ ordering as the P3 cross-tie, and the two self-application rendering results. The Discussion section locates the empirical evidence in the boundary-condition table the companion theory specifies, surfaces the iterative-cohort-growth protocol as a longitudinal P4 self-test, and notes the β-conditional Rec\*(β) cross-tie as a footnote rather than a primary contribution. A Companion Computation Script subsection names the public-mirror code paths and run commands for reproducibility. A Conclusion section, Limitations, and Acknowledgments close the paper.

## Theory

This section restates only the elements of Zharnikov's (2026ao) Operator-role theory that Paper B's empirical demonstration directly extends, and grounds the empirical claims in recent GenAI / knowledge-management scholarship. Full theoretical apparatus lives in the companion paper.

### *Grounding in GenAI and knowledge-management scholarship.*

The empirical demonstration this paper reports sits at the intersection of three recent strands of GenAI / knowledge-management scholarship. Alavi, Leidner, and Mousavi (2024) revisit Alavi and Leidner's (2001) foundational lens in the generative-AI era and observe that the traditional KM dichotomy between codification and personalization strategies no longer organizes the field cleanly because the substrate becomes machine-tractable in ways neither strategy anticipated. Paper B's structural test of rendering-equivalence operationalizes that observation at the artifact level: two prose renderings of a shared underlying spine share Rec = 4 linked propositions with preserved antecedents on both available management-theory twin pairs; the substrate is what AI projection can verify, the rendering is what human projection retains responsibility for. The KM-perspective frame supplies the field-level reading; the typed-DAG isomorphism analysis supplies the artifact-level evidence.

Benbya, Strich, and Tamm (2024) catalog GenAI promises (productivity, augmentation, novel knowledge creation) and perils (hallucination, deskilling, opaque provenance) for knowledge and creative work. Paper B's bibliographic-hallucination audit (~83% hallucination rate on 12 AI-suggested anchors; see §Method §Phase 1 anchor verification) is one concrete instantiation of the perils side of the taxonomy at the citation-substrate boundary. The cross-operator extraction discipline (Operator B Claude renderer ≠ Operator C GPT-4o extractor; see §Method §Cross-operator extraction discipline) is the methodological response: within-model memory contamination is empirically bounded at ~15 percentage-points strict / ~0 semantic when extraction is delegated to a different model than the one that produced the rendering. Both findings concretize the Benbya-Strich-Tamm taxonomy with measurable artifact-level evidence.

The productivity-heterogeneity empirical work in Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) — generative AI raises productivity asymmetrically across worker skill levels, with the largest gains for less-experienced workers — supplies a complementary empirical template for the cost-asymmetry mechanism Paper A's P3 specifies. Where Brynjolfsson et al. measure productivity heterogeneity at the worker-task level, Paper B measures structural-substrate preservation at the artifact level: both report that the post-AI shift is unevenly distributed across the layers where AI projection is asked to operate, and both report that the partition between substrate-tractable operations and judgment operations matters for whether AI projection delivers on its promises.

Kirchner, Bolisani, Kassaneh, Scarso, and Taraghi (2025) bring a technology-organization-environment (TOE) qualitative grounding from software-development practice: knowledge-management workflows under GenAI converge on a substrate-and-rendering split that mirrors the L → S → R decomposition Zharnikov (2026ao) formalizes. Their qualitative observations on how software development teams adopt GenAI tools — substrate maintenance (code as substrate; documentation as rendering; AI verification at substrate sub-linear cost; human judgment at rendering super-linear cost) — supply contextual triangulation for Paper B's claim that the substrate-vs-rendering distinction is operationally tractable beyond the academic-publishing case the validation cases illustrate. George, Storey, and Hong (2025) extend the knowledge-anchor framing to AI-mediated learning environments, observing that ChatGPT functions as a knowledge anchor whose substrate (the queried knowledge representation) shapes downstream cohort renderings; the framing reads cleanly through the Operator-role / three-layer apparatus this paper empirically demonstrates.

### *Three-level decomposition (inherited).*

Every knowledge artifact under the companion theory's boundary conditions has three layers. The log substrate L is an append-only sequence of authoring events. The semantic substrate or **spine** S is the directed labeled graph σ(L) extracted from L; vertices are typed claims, observations, methods, findings, propositions, derivations, rivals, robustness checks, limitations, and assumption atoms; edges are the typed relations among them. The rendering R is the cohort-conditional prose produced as ρ(S, audience, language, medium). The extraction operator σ is lossy; the rendering operator ρ is cohort-dependent and reconstructable.

### *P4 — rendering-equivalence under spine-preservation (the proposition this paper demonstrates).*

For any artifact whose spine is locked, two prose renderings of the same spine arrive at the same set of conclusions if and only if both renderings preserve the spine's structural elements. The conclusion set of a rendering R is defined as K(R) := σ(R) ∩ S, the σ-extracted typed-DAG restricted to the locked subset of S — propositions, antecedent edges, evidence anchors, and boundary conditions explicit in the locked spine. Renderings may differ on every surface dimension (language, audience register, structural ordering, tone, formatting, length, medium) and still converge on conclusions. P4 holds as a preservation result under axiom A1 (σ-faithfulness on locked subsets): when a rendering preserves the locked elements, the σ extraction operator recovers them. Axiom A1 is empirically testable via inter-coder reliability on retroactive spine extractions, with κ ≥ .75 on both node typing and edge placement as the operational threshold. Below threshold, A1 fails empirically on the affected subdomain and P4 degrades from a preservation result to a probabilistic tendency on that subdomain.

The companion theory's Online Appendix D supplies the historical existence proof for P4 on the founding papers of quantum mechanics: Heisenberg (1925) matrix-mechanics rendering and Schrödinger (1926) wave-mechanics rendering of (what Schrödinger 1926 equivalence paper and von Neumann 1932 later proved is) the same underlying spine, in incompatible formalisms, converging on the same conclusions. Paper B's empirical contribution is to extend the P4 demonstration into contemporary management research at the management-theory twin-pair level. Each twin pair is treated as two prose renderings of a shared substrate, and the typed-DAG recombination metric Rec(G₁, G₂) measures the extent of substrate-sharing between them.

### *The recombination metric (inherited; central to P4 operationalization).*

Given two spines G₁ and G₂, the recombination operator Rec(G₁, G₂) is the size of the maximum common subgraph that preserves both node typing and antecedent edges (Zharnikov 2026ao). Under the P4 reading, Rec(G₁, G₂) measures the structural-substrate preservation between two prose renderings: a high Rec value on independently-authored prose renderings of a shared subject matter is direct evidence that the two prose renderings preserve a common underlying substrate. The companion theory's deterministic threshold Rec ≥ 3 with preserved antecedents is the operational criterion for "the two renderings share a substrate" at the level the typed-DAG schema can detect. Paper B's central empirical task is to compute Rec on real management-theory twin pairs and ask whether the threshold is met. (Distinct from this P4 reading, the companion theory's P2 — organizational recombination rates — uses Rec as input to a separate large-N organizational-outcome prediction; that proposition is not tested at this release.)

### *Cost-function specification (inherited; secondary in this paper).*

The companion theory specifies that AI verification cost on the spine scales sub-linearly in node count, c_AI(S) = α · |V|^β with β < 1, and human verification cost on a rendering scales super-linearly in token count, c_human(R) = γ · |tokens|^δ with δ > 1. The structural asymmetry β < 1 < δ is what makes the Operator-role partition between structural-substrate operations (allocated to AI projection) and judgment operations (retained by human projection) economically dominant. Paper B reports grid-anchored illustrative estimates of β and δ at the parameter-grid level Zharnikov (2026ao) Online Appendix C specifies (β ∈ {.5, .7, .9} and δ ∈ {1.1, 1.3, 1.5}). Under current scope, the β/δ estimates supply supportive evidence for the joint ordering — not a falsificational test of P3 at the organizational-outcome scale, which is reserved for v4.0.0.

### *Boundary conditions (inherited).*

The companion theory applies under joint sufficient conditions C1 (authoring is version-controlled), C2 (claims are propositional rather than purely narrative), C3 (audience-schema divergence is non-trivial), and C4 (artifact volume exceeds unaided human reviewer verification capacity). Paper B's twin pairs satisfy C1 through C4: both members of each pair are version-controlled, propositional, addressed to audience-divergence non-trivial relative to their disciplinary origin, and produced in a literature whose volume exceeds reviewer capacity at the discipline level. Paper B further specifies a pair-specific boundary condition PB_BC1 (revised under the P4 reframe): both twin-pair source papers are produced as separate prose renderings of an extractable substrate. Whether the authors were aware of each other's drafts at composition time (Bikard-protocol independence) is helpful for distinguishing rendering-equivalence from textual copying but not essential for the P4 demonstration — P4 asks whether two prose renderings share a substrate, not whether the authors were independently working.

### *Positioning against adjacent strategy literatures.*

The rendering-equivalence claim Paper B demonstrates sits at the intersection of several adjacent literatures whose treatment of knowledge convergence and recombination supplies the theoretical context the present paper extends.

*Recombinant search and knowledge representation*. Kaplan and Vakili (2015) show that breakthrough innovation in patents involves recombination of components from distant knowledge domains, and develop the topic-modeling-based measurement that distinguishes substantive recombination from surface-level vocabulary overlap. Paper B's recombination metric Rec is a complementary measurement at the typed-DAG-substrate level rather than the topic-distribution level: where Kaplan and Vakili measure recombination as a distance metric on the latent topic space of a patent corpus, Rec measures the size of the maximum common subgraph preserving node typing and antecedent edges across two structural extractions. The two measurements are not substitutes; the topic-distribution distance is sensitive to surface vocabulary overlap (which Rec deliberately excludes by abstracting to typed nodes) while Rec is sensitive to whether the underlying claims-and-antecedents structure is preserved across renderings.

*Boundary objects and knowledge transfer*. Carlile (2004) distinguishes syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic boundaries to knowledge transfer across organizational boundaries and develops the boundary-object construct as the artifact that supports transfer. Paper B's spine-vs-rendering distinction operates at a finer grain: each rendering is a knowledge artifact crossing an audience boundary (Carlile's pragmatic level), and the spine is the boundary object's typed structural representation. The empirical question Paper B asks — whether two prose renderings of the same spine converge on conclusions — is a precondition for boundary-object effectiveness at Carlile's pragmatic level. The rendering-equivalence result extends Carlile's framework by supplying a structural test for whether a candidate boundary object preserves the substrate the senders intend.

*Automation–augmentation paradox*. Raisch and Krakowski (2021) frame the choice between automation (AI replacing humans) and augmentation (AI extending humans) as the central paradox of AI in management. The Operator role Zharnikov (2026ao) develops resolves the paradox at the role level rather than the task level: the Operator's intrinsic structural-substrate operations are appropriately allocated to AI projection regardless of whether automation or augmentation is the framing, and the intrinsic judgment operations remain on the human projection. Paper B's empirical contribution shows that the structural-substrate allocation is operationally tractable on real management-theory artifacts — the substrate is extractable, comparable across renderings, and reproducible at the typed-DAG level the AI projection can verify.

*Resource origins and search*. Felin, Kauffman, and Zenger (2023) argue that strategic resources originate from theory-driven search rather than scarcity-driven discovery, and locate the locus of value creation in the theoretical apparatus that identifies which resources to combine. Paper B's evidence is consistent with this: the substrate of a strategic theory paper is a theory-of-search-targets, and two prose renderings of the same substrate converge on the same set of recommended search targets even when their vocabularies differ. The Rec metric quantifies the structural overlap that Felin et al.'s framework implies should exist between researchers reaching similar conclusions via independent theoretical paths.

*Organizational learning antecedents*. Levitt and March (1988) supply the organizational-learning antecedent that both Eisenhardt-Martin and Zollo-Winter inherit at the proposition level of the focal twin pair (L2 in §Results). Levinthal and March (1993) extend the learning-myopia analysis to the strategic-search regime where exploration and exploitation trade off, and supply the antecedent positioning that bridges to the knowledge-based-view pair's K1/K3 substrate elements on knowledge integration and tacit-vs-explicit knowledge.

The framework's core constructs — the Operator role, the era-dependent projection composition table, the typed-DAG node-and-edge-type catalog, the preservation theorem under axiom A1, the spine-first drafting protocol — are developed at depth in the companion paper (Zharnikov 2026ao). Paper B inherits each by namespaced reference (paper_a:operator_role, paper_a:appendix_A_schema, paper_a:appendix_B_protocol, paper_a:P1, paper_a:P4, paper_a:axiom_A1) and extends the framework empirically.

## Method

### *Twin-pair selection.*

Paper B selects two pairs of independently-authored management-theory papers, each treated as two prose renderings of a shared substrate. The focal pair is Eisenhardt and Martin (2000) *Strategic Management Journal* and Zollo and Winter (2002) *Organization Science* on the formation and evolution of dynamic capabilities. The knowledge-based-view pair is Grant (1996) and Liebeskind (1996) in the *Strategic Management Journal* Winter Special Issue "Knowledge and the Firm." Both pairs satisfy the pair-specific boundary condition PB_BC1 — each pair consists of two independently-published source papers whose spines are retroactively extractable, with traceable antecedent edges back into the shared antecedent literature. Bikard's (2000) idea-twin protocol, which identifies independently-authored pairs as natural experiments in cross-formalism convergence on a shared idea, is a useful guide for distinguishing rendering-equivalence from textual copying or strict extension; the focal pair is at the partial-independence end of the Bikard spectrum (Eisenhardt and Martin's reference list cites Zollo and Winter's 1999 working-paper predecessor, though the in-text engagement does not build Eisenhardt and Martin's central thesis on the prior preprint), and the KBV pair is at the strong-independence end (same Special Issue parallel submission with confirmed mutual non-citation at draft time). Both serve the P4 demonstration: each pair's two prose renderings are unambiguously distinct prose products, and the question is whether they share a substrate at the typed-DAG level.

### *Standalone precursor (Tushman and O'Reilly 1996).*

A standalone single-paper spine extraction of Tushman and O'Reilly's (1996) *California Management Review* ambidextrous-organizations formulation is retained from the current release. The extraction was originally scoped to pair with Duncan's (1976) North-Holland book chapter; the Duncan (1976) chapter PDF could not be sourced. The Tushman and O'Reilly (1996) spine is released as a standalone precursor that supplies one of the five β/δ calibration points and remains available for any future second rendering of its substrate.

### *Retroactive spine extraction.*

For each of the five available source papers (Eisenhardt and Martin 2000; Zollo and Winter 2002; Tushman and O'Reilly 1996; Grant 1996; Liebeskind 1996), the operator performed retroactive spine extraction per the spine-first drafting protocol (Zharnikov 2026ao Online Appendix B) applied in reverse. The operator read the source paper, identified its central propositions, observations, methods, and findings, classified each into the typed-DAG schema (10 node types; 17 edge types per Zharnikov 2026ao Online Appendix A), and traced antecedent edges to the foundational literature each proposition depended on. The extracted spines are published as VALIDATION_CASE_PB_*.yaml files alongside this paper.

Extraction at this release is single-coder by the author. Inter-coder reliability κ measurement on axiom A1 (σ-faithfulness on locked subsets), with κ ≥ .75 the operational threshold below which P4 degrades from preservation result to probabilistic tendency, is pre-registered for the next release task δ; current release-version reliability rests on the operator's spine-first-protocol training and the published-spine artifacts that future coders can inspect and replicate.

### *Recombination metric computation as P4 operationalization.*

For each twin pair, the operator computed Rec(G₁, G₂) per Zharnikov (2026ao) — the size of the maximum common subgraph preserving both node typing and antecedent edges. Under the P4 reading, Rec(G₁, G₂) measures the structural-substrate preservation between the two prose renderings: a Rec value at or above the deterministic threshold of three with preserved antecedents is direct evidence that the two prose renderings share a substrate at the typed-DAG level. Computation logic is implemented at [`code/rec_metric.py`](https://github.com/spectralbranding/meaningfulness-papers/blob/main/meaning-meaningfulness-empirical/code/rec_metric.py) in the public mirror and runs deterministically on the YAML-encoded spines.

### *Random-graph null baseline for Rec.*

To test whether the observed Rec = 4 on each twin pair is non-trivial under a random null, the paper computes a random-shadow null distribution. For each real twin-pair member (G_EM and G_Grant), the operator generates 1,000 random "shadow spines" matched on (a) node count, (b) node-type distribution (all nodes typed as propositions per the focal-pair structural extraction), and (c) edge-type distribution (drawn uniformly from Zharnikov 2026ao's 17-edge catalog), but with antecedent edges drawn at random from the pooled set of 20 antecedent references appearing across the five real Paper B spines (Nelson and Winter 1982; Levitt and March 1988; Teece-Pisano-Shuen 1997; Eisenhardt-Tabrizi 1995; Penrose 1959; Barney 1991; Coase 1937; Williamson 1975; Polanyi 1966; Levinthal and March 1993; and others). For each shadow, the operator computes Rec(real, shadow) per the simplified maximum-common-subgraph rule that counts matched-antecedent + matched-edge-type pairs across the two structures, capping at the smaller spine's node count. The shadow generation and Rec computation are implemented at [`code/null_baseline.py`](https://github.com/spectralbranding/meaningfulness-papers/blob/main/meaning-meaningfulness-empirical/code/null_baseline.py) in the public mirror and execute deterministically under fixed seed 42. The null-baseline is reported alongside the observed Rec values in §Results.

### *Cost-function exponent calibration (secondary).*

For each of the five extracted spines, the operator estimated β by recording AI-projection verification cost as a function of spine node count |V|, calibrated against a fixed AI-projection harness (Claude Opus 4.7 in topological-order traversal mode, fixed temperature 0, with provenance verification at each node). For each of the five source-paper renderings, the operator estimated δ by recording human-projection verification cost as a function of token count, calibrated against the operator's own coding-time logs at fixed prose-reading-and-tagging pace. Both estimates are reported as illustrative point values without confidence intervals; statistical-power claims are out of scope. The calibration harness is implemented at [`code/cost_function_calibration.py`](https://github.com/spectralbranding/meaningfulness-papers/blob/main/meaning-meaningfulness-empirical/code/cost_function_calibration.py) in the public mirror. The β/δ ordering is a secondary contribution under current scope, supportive of the companion theory's P3 ordering but not a falsificational test of P3 at the organizational-outcome scale.

The single-operator δ calibration is a known limitation; we address it in the Threats-to-Reliability subsection and schedule a third-party-coder upgrade for a future release.

### *Cross-operator extraction discipline.*

The P4 evidence in §Results §Self-application of P4 (the Substack rendering of the paper's own substrate, the LinkedIn rendering of the focal-pair shared substrate, and the cross-paper rendering of the companion theory's full theoretical apparatus) is collected under a three-operator pipeline that separates rendering from extraction. Operator A is the orchestrator that supplies the source spine, compares results, and reports preservation. Operator B is the rendering operator that receives the source spine and produces prose at the target audience-register. Operator C is the extraction operator that receives ONLY the prose and the appendix-A codebook (10 node types + 17 edge types) — never the source spine — and produces an extracted spine for comparison against the source. Operator B and Operator C must be DIFFERENT models; using the same model for both produces "extraction" that is partly within-model memory reconstruction rather than pure prose-to-spine inference, confounding the preservation result with context memory.

In the present paper, Operator A is the author (orchestrator + comparator) and Claude Opus (in-fleet authoring environment); Operator B is Claude Opus (renderer) for the three Session H renderings produced in May 2026; Operator C is GPT-4o (extractor) for the cross-operator preservation result. Where earlier within-operator Claude-self-extracted results are reported, they are presented alongside the cross-operator GPT-4o-extracted results so that reviewers can compare within-model memory contribution to extraction. The within-vs-cross delta is itself a methodological finding about the magnitude of within-model context memory in rendering→extraction pipelines. Full per-locked-item cross-operator preservation tables at `CROSS_OPERATOR_EXTRACTION_RESULTS.md`. The within-operator and cross-operator results are jointly reported in §Results §Self-application.

## Discussion

### *Reliability of the P4 Demonstration.*

The central P4 demonstration rests on the typed-DAG isomorphism analyses in `TWIN_PAIR_ISOMORPHISM_PB_FOCAL.md` and `TWIN_PAIR_ISOMORPHISM_PB_KBV.md`, plus the two self-application analyses in `SELF_APPLICATION_ISOMORPHISM_PB.md` (Substack practitioner rendering) and `TWIN_PAIR_ISOMORPHISM_PB_FOCAL.md` §8 (LinkedIn third rendering of the focal-pair substrate). Three reliability considerations bound the strength of the demonstration at this release.

- **Single-coder extraction across all five source spines and both self-application renderings.** The four source-paper spines plus the two re-extracted self-application spines reflect a single coder's typing decisions. Per the companion theory's axiom A1 (σ-faithfulness on locked subsets), κ ≥ .75 on both node typing and antecedent-edge placement is the operational threshold for the rendering-equivalence prediction to hold as a preservation result rather than a probabilistic tendency. The next release pre-registers an independent second-coder pass on two of the five source spines (Eisenhardt and Martin 2000 from the focal pair; Grant 1996 from the KBV pair) per `PHASE4_KAPPA_MEASUREMENT_2026-05-27.md`; the codebook (paper_a:appendix_A_schema), sampling frame, and falsifier specification are locked alongside this this release. Axiom A1 reliability is currently asserted on the basis of the published-spine artifacts; the measurement lands before any tier-1 venue submission. Below threshold on either dimension, P4 degrades from preservation result to probabilistic tendency on the affected subdomain.
- **Retroactive extraction.** Each source-paper spine is a retroactive reconstruction; the original authors did not produce their papers under a spine-first protocol. Reviewers may dispute whether what is extracted is "their substrate" or "the present author's projection of their substrate." The mitigation is that the extracted spines are published as YAML files alongside this paper, so any second coder can inspect node typing and edge placement directly and challenge specific choices. The self-application renderings (Substack and LinkedIn) are exempt from this concern because they are produced explicitly under the spine-first protocol with the source spine available; their reliability concern is the same-operator re-extraction issue the next-release κ pass addresses on a different subdomain.
- **Bare-content degradation of P4.** The preservation result holds as stated under the strict K-as-typed-DAG definition of conclusions. Under a weaker bare-propositional reading, the ← direction of P4 degrades to a high-probability empirical tendency rather than an iff. The framework takes the strict definition as central; Paper B's Rec values and self-application preservation rates are computed against the strict typed-DAG reading.

The self-application Substack rendering of the paper's own substrate (Task α; 11/14 strict preservation, 14/14 semantic preservation) and the LinkedIn third rendering of the focal-pair shared substrate (Task β; 4/4 preservation with antecedent edges) extend the P4 evidence base with two additional renderings of substrates already in the corpus. Task γ (cross-language re-rendering of the paper's abstract into Russian) is pre-registered for the next release alongside the κ measurement; together the two next-release tasks address the cross-language and cross-coder boundaries this release does not cross.

### *Methodological refinement note: strict-match vs semantic-equivalence on the appendix-A schema.*

The strict-preservation rates reported across the Phase 2, Phase 2.5, and Phase 3 renderings (11/14, 12/15, 4/4 respectively) sit consistently below the semantic-preservation rates (14/14, 15/15, 4/4) on the same locked sets. Across all three rendering boundaries, zero substrate items are contradicted; the strict-vs-semantic gap reflects propositions that preserve semantically but fail the strict node-typing-AND-edge match criterion the appendix-A schema imposes. The propositions that pass semantic but fail strict are systematically of three kinds: (a) organizational-outcome-statement metadata in formal-prediction propositions (compressed to operational-direction claims at practitioner register); (b) the log-layer or version-trajectory components of methodology-spec propositions (compressed away when the rendering's audience does not need the version-control metadata); (c) cost-function functional-form mathematics (compressed to sub-linear / super-linear plain-English ordering when the audience cannot evaluate functional notation).

The pattern is consistent enough to warrant a methodological refinement candidate for future schema work: the appendix-A schema's strict-match criterion may be over-precise for cross-register renderings; semantic-equivalence relaxation may be warranted for some node types (organizational-outcome-component, log-layer-component, functional-form-component). The framework can plausibly predict in advance which node types will compress at which rendering boundaries, and a future spine-schema extension could add per-node "preservation-class" tags ({formal-required, semantic-sufficient, optional}) to make this prediction explicit. Within this release the observation stands as a finding about schema precision-granularity — not a P4 falsifier, not a framework limitation, but a refinement candidate the framework's own structure predicts.

### *Reframing of academic scholarly communities as a focal organizational form.*

Academic scholarly communities are organizations under the companion theory's boundary conditions C1 through C4: their authoring is version-controlled (preprint repositories, journal submission systems); their claims are propositional (the focal pair illustrates this directly); audience-schema divergence is non-trivial (the venue split between practitioner-tier outlets and academic-tier journals); and artifact volume exceeds reviewer capacity at the discipline level. Paper B treats academic communities as the focal organizational form for v1.0.0 through v3.0.0; v2.0.0+ adds non-academic renderings (consulting white papers; corporate strategy documents rendered from a shared organizational substrate) to test generalization across organizational forms.

### *What this release does not establish.*

Paper B v1.0.0 does *not* establish: a falsificational test of P2 (organizational recombination rates) at the citation-weighted-derivative-work scale; a falsificational test of P3 at the organizational-outcome scale; large-N statistical-power claims on β/δ; generalization of the P4 demonstration beyond academic scholarly communities; or recombination-rate predictions Rec\*(β) tests at the prospective lab-cohort scale. Each is pre-registered in the versioning trajectory above with a release-version commitment.

### *Practical implications for organizations.*

The central implication of the P4 demonstration is operational. If two prose renderings of the same substrate converge on conclusions whenever both preserve the substrate's structural elements — and Paper B's evidence is that they do, on contemporary management theory — then organizations producing rendered knowledge artifacts at volume have an operationally meaningful question to ask: does our authoring discipline preserve substrate at the typed-DAG level the spine-first protocol can audit? Organizations whose internal knowledge artifacts pass the Rec ≥ 3 with preserved antecedents bar across paired renderings (e.g., a strategy memo rendered for the executive team and a derivative one-pager rendered for a board sub-committee) are producing substrate-preserving renderings the AI-projection of the Operator can verify and recombine; those whose paired renderings fall below the threshold are producing artifacts where verification cost and recombination uncertainty concentrate on the human-projection side. The Substack practitioner-register article that task α produces (and the iterative working-paper format Paper B exemplifies) is one worked instance of substrate-preserving multi-rendering at the individual-author scale; organizations operating Operator roles at team scale can instrument analogous internal harnesses.

The β/δ ordering reported above provides a secondary anchor for cost-channel discussions. Organizations operating under post-2023 generative-AI capability (Cell A in the boundary table; estimated β ∈ [.5, .9] from Paper B's small-N evidence) face the substrate-verification cost profile the companion theory predicts. Internal organizational extension of the β/δ calibration to a specific firm's substrate and rendering harnesses is more durable than the point estimates Paper B reports; the methodological contribution is how to instrument the calibration, not the specific values.

The empirical evidence base carries three concrete implications for downstream empirical work. *First*, verification at scale and cohort selection: the cross-operator extraction discipline (Operator B ≠ Operator C) gives a methodological template for any future P4 testing across larger N. The within-vs-cross-operator preservation delta is the methodological finding that bounds within-model memory contamination at ~15 percentage-points strict / ~0 semantic; future work scaling beyond two twin pairs should report both within-operator and cross-operator preservation rates to make the comparison cumulative. *Second*, twin-pair selection criteria: the focal pair (EM 2000 + ZW 2002) has partial Bikard-protocol independence (EM cites a 1999 ZW working paper); the KBV pair (Grant 1996 + Liebeskind 1996) has strong Bikard independence (same SMJ Winter 1996 Special Issue; mutual non-citation). The next-release pair selection should target strong Bikard independence as the operational criterion to make the rendering-equivalence reading distinguishable from textual influence. *Third*, cross-LLM coordination in research workflows: the Benbya, Strich, and Tamm (2024) GenAI taxonomy resolves cleanly under this paper's reading — operators that route AI projection to substrate-tractable operations while reserving judgment for human projection (or for a different operator) capture the promises and avoid the perils; the cross-operator extraction discipline operationalizes this routing inside research workflows. The Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) productivity-heterogeneity findings supply the worker-task-level template for what a future Operator-role productivity study would measure.

## Conclusion

The central claim of this paper is empirically supported at this release: two independently-authored prose renderings of a shared underlying structural substrate share four linked propositions with preserved antecedents on both available pairs (Eisenhardt and Martin 2000 + Zollo and Winter 2002 on dynamic capabilities; Grant 1996 + Liebeskind 1996 on the knowledge-based view of the firm). The observed Rec = 4 values sit above the 99th percentile of a 1,000-shadow random null distribution; the rendering-equivalence pattern is not attributable to chance under that null. Two additional renderings of substrates already in the corpus — a practitioner-register Substack rendering of the paper's own structure and a LinkedIn rendering of the focal-pair shared substrate — preserve the substrate across the academic → practitioner register boundary at 79% strict / 100% semantic and 100% / 100% respectively, with zero contradictions across renderings.

This evidence base extends Zharnikov's (2026ao) P4 (rendering-equivalence under spine-preservation) from the Heisenberg–Schrödinger historical existence proof into contemporary strategy research at small N. The framework's secondary prediction P3 (verification-cost asymmetry with β < 1 < δ) receives supportive evidence from the five-spine, five-rendering grid-anchored β/δ ordering, without claiming a falsificational test at the organizational-outcome scale; the falsificational P3 test is the v4.0.0 scope of the iterative-cohort-growth trajectory pre-registered alongside this release.

The paper places its framework in conversation with recombinant-search scholarship (Kaplan and Vakili 2015), boundary-object theory (Carlile 2004), the automation–augmentation paradox (Raisch and Krakowski 2021), resource-origins theory (Felin, Kauffman, and Zenger 2023), and organizational-learning antecedents (Levinthal and March 1993; Levitt and March 1988). The Rec metric is positioned as a complementary measurement to topic-distribution-distance approaches: where topic-distribution distance is sensitive to surface vocabulary, Rec is sensitive to whether the underlying claims-and-antecedents structure is preserved.

Three commitments are pre-registered for the next release: an inter-coder κ measurement on axiom A1 with κ ≥ .75 the operational threshold (codebook and sampling frame locked at `PHASE4_KAPPA_MEASUREMENT_2026-05-27.md`); a cross-language re-rendering test (Russian) of the paper's abstract or §Theory; and the integration of next-release results into the next paper release. Below the κ threshold, P4 degrades from a preservation result to a probabilistic tendency on the affected subdomain — an honest empirical boundary the framework names in advance.

The paper's broader argument is that rendering-equivalence under spine-preservation is operationally tractable on real management-theory artifacts and, by extension, on the propositional knowledge artifacts organizations produce at volume in a generative-AI era. The structural substrate of a knowledge artifact is a first-class object distinct from its prose rendering; the substrate is comparable across renderings, reproducible at the typed-DAG level, and amenable to AI verification at the sub-linear cost the framework's β specification names. Organizations that maintain explicit, version-controlled substrates of their decision-relevant knowledge artifacts can route AI to the substrate and reserve human judgment for the rendering, capturing both the cost-asymmetry advantage P3 names and the conclusion-convergence guarantee P4 names. The methodological apparatus this paper develops at the level of strategy theory's own historical record is the same apparatus organizations can instrument internally on their own artifacts.

## Limitations

This release is honest about scope. Seven limitations carry forward to future versions per the trajectory documented above:

- Two twin pairs (= four prose renderings) plus two self-application renderings is small-N P4 demonstration, not falsification at scale. The cross-pair convergence at Rec = 4 across two sub-domains is suggestive but not conclusive at this scale; v2.0.0 expansion to a larger pair corpus and prospective renderings tests whether the demonstration generalizes.
- Single-coder extraction across all five source spines and both self-application renderings. Inter-coder reliability κ measurement on axiom A1 is pre-registered at the next release per `PHASE4_KAPPA_MEASUREMENT_2026-05-27.md`; the codebook (paper_a:appendix_A_schema), sampling frame (Eisenhardt and Martin 2000 from the focal pair; Grant 1996 from the KBV pair), and falsifier specification (κ ≥ .75 on both node typing and antecedent-edge placement) are locked alongside this this release. Axiom A1 reliability is currently asserted; the measurement lands before any tier-1 venue submission. Below κ = .75 on either dimension, P4 degrades from preservation result to probabilistic tendency on the affected subdomain per the companion theory's axiom A1 specification.
- The KBV-pair extraction depends on OCR of the Grant (1996) PDF (image-only source). Small text-fidelity errors at the margin are possible; the next-release second-coder pass on the Grant 1996 spine, optionally supported by a Perplexity-based verbatim-quote verification step, addresses this.
- Author-self-timing for δ introduces a demand-effect vulnerability the next-release protocol does not directly remediate (the second-coder pass addresses extraction reliability for Rec, not the rendering-verification timing for the cost-function δ calibration). A third-party-coder substitution for δ is scheduled for v2.0.0.
- Empirical evidence concentrates in Cell A of the boundary table (high task codifiability × high AI-generation quality); Cells B–D remain out of scope at this release.
- The Tushman and O'Reilly (1996) standalone single-paper extraction has no second rendering of its substrate at this release. Candidate second renderings include the O'Reilly and Tushman (2013) *Academy of Management Perspectives* retrospective review, which renders the ambidexterity substrate seventeen years later for a different audience. future releases may take up this second rendering extraction.
- The Substack and LinkedIn self-application renderings are operator-produced; the Phase 2 strict-preservation rate of 79% on the Substack rendering is the upper-bound estimate under same-operator extraction. The next-release κ measurement is the independent test of the axiom A1 reliability that supports the strict-preservation reading.
