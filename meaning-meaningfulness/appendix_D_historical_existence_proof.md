## D.1 The Heisenberg–Schrödinger twin pair

### Background

In 1925, Werner Heisenberg published *Über quantentheoretische Umdeutung kinematischer und mechanischer Beziehungen* in *Zeitschrift für Physik* (vol. 33, pp. 879–893), introducing matrix mechanics through a reformulation that retained only observable quantities. Max Born and Pascual Jordan extended the matrix formulation later in 1925 in the same journal (vol. 34, pp. 858–888); Born, Heisenberg, and Jordan completed the apparatus in 1926 in the "Three-Men paper" *Zur Quantenmechanik II* (vol. 35, pp. 557–615).

In late 1925 and early 1926, Erwin Schrödinger arrived independently at a wave-mechanics formulation through the *Quantisierung als Eigenwertproblem* series of four papers in *Annalen der Physik* (vol. 79–81). The wave-mechanics formulation posited a wave equation whose eigenvalues recover the atomic energy levels matrix mechanics computes through its non-commuting-matrix apparatus.

The two formulations are mathematically incompatible at the surface. Matrix mechanics operates on infinite-dimensional matrices and non-commuting operators in an abstract algebraic space; wave mechanics operates on continuous wavefunctions in coordinate space with partial-differential-equation eigenvalue methods. The two operators (Heisenberg and Schrödinger) had no methodological agreement and no formalism overlap.

Yet both formulations predicted the same atomic spectra. Schrödinger himself, in *Über das Verhältnis der Heisenberg-Born-Jordanschen Quantenmechanik zu der meinen* (*Annalen der Physik* 1926, vol. 79, pp. 734–756), proved that matrix mechanics and wave mechanics are mathematically equivalent. John von Neumann, in *Mathematische Grundlagen der Quantenmechanik* (Springer 1932), supplied the rigorous Hilbert-space functional-analytic foundation showing that the two formulations are unitarily equivalent representations of the same underlying physical theory.

### Pauli 1927 as within-author rendering-equivalence

Wolfgang Pauli's 1927 paper *Zur Quantenmechanik des magnetischen Elektrons* (*Zeitschrift für Physik* vol. 43, pp. 601–623) strengthens the historical-record evidence. Pauli derives the spin-electron problem in both formalisms within a single paper. He uses the matrix-mechanics treatment of spin in the Heisenberg–Jordan style first; he then derives the same problem using the Schrödinger wave-mechanics method; and he states explicitly that the wave-mechanics derivation produces results "completely equivalent in their consequences to the matrix equations of Heisenberg and Jordan." This is a within-author rendering-equivalence demonstration — same operator performing both renderings within one paper and stating equivalence — which removes the coordination-or-cross-influence concerns that cross-author cases (Heisenberg vs Schrödinger as separate operators) cannot rule out.
