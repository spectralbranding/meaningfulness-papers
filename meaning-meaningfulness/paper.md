Dmitry Zharnikov

## Abstract

Generative AI shifts the cost structure of organizational knowledge work asymmetrically across the layers of the artifact it produces. The structural substrate — the typed graph of propositions, dependencies, and evidence anchors — becomes cheap to construct, verify, and recombine; the cohort-conditional prose rendering does not, and may become relatively more costly. This paper introduces the **Operator** as the role-level abstraction that absorbs the shift: a role with intrinsic structural-substrate operations and intrinsic judgment operations, whose human-versus-AI projection composition varies by era. The role-as-projection move extends the automation-augmentation paradox from task allocation to role-level division of operations. Two propositions follow. P1 (separability): structural substrate and rendering are independently optimizable. P4 (rendering-equivalence under spine-preservation): two renderings of a locked substrate converge on conclusions if and only if both preserve its structural elements, under a faithful extraction axiom. The paper illustrates the framework on a management-theory twin pair (dynamic capabilities) and applies it reflexively to its own production. Empirical estimation of two additional predictions about recombination and cost-asymmetry parameters is reserved for a companion paper. Three Design Propositions follow for organizational governance, role and incentive design, and editorial-process decoupling.

**Keywords**: generative AI, role theory, knowledge-based view of the firm, boundary objects, automation-augmentation, cost asymmetry, organizational design, rendering-equivalence.

---

The arrival of capable generative AI inside the workflow of scholarly knowledge production has surfaced a problem that prior management theory has not had occasion to address directly. Generative models cost-asymmetrically across the layers of the artifacts knowledge workers produce: the structural substrate of a paper or report — its claims, dependencies, evidence anchors, and rivals — is computationally tractable, while the cohort-conditional rendering of that substrate into prose for a specific reader is comparatively more expensive on the same compute budget. Practitioner observation has surfaced the phenomenon (Kulkarni et al. 2024); methodological reflection from inside management research has flagged the implications (Koçak, Levinthal, and Puranam 2023; von Krogh, Roberson, and Gruber 2023); and the automation-augmentation literature has begun to theorize the task-level allocation problem (Raisch and Krakowski 2021). What is missing is a role-level theory of how the cost asymmetry restructures the operator who produces the artifact — researcher, analyst, technical writer, knowledge-management practitioner — and what division of labor between substrate maintenance and judgment that restructure implies.

We extend role theory to the AI-projection condition. The primary contribution is the **Operator** as a role-level abstraction above the human-versus-AI instance distinction. The Operator's intrinsic operations split into two classes with qualitatively different projection patterns. *Structural substrate operations* — extraction of observations, methods, and findings; DAG and edge-type maintenance; consistency checking; surfacing rivals; robustness checks; draft generation from substrate — are era-dependent: pre-AI by human projection, post-AI typically by AI projection under human review. *Judgment operations* — selection of substrate slices to query, fork-rebase-merge decisions on surfaced claims, cohort-specific rendering, verification that substrate operations reflect operator intent — are era-invariant and stay with human projection across all current and near-future composition modes. The role-as-projection move extends the automation-augmentation paradox (Raisch and Krakowski 2021) from task-level allocation to role-level division between intrinsic operations and era-dependent projections, and inherits the conjoined-agency framing Murray, Rhymer, and Sirmon (2021) develop for human-and-technological agency forms in organizations. The role-theoretic move sits in the established lineage of role-coordination theory (Bailey and Barley 2010; Bechky and Okhuysen 2011), algorithmic-control scholarship (Kellogg, Valentine, and Christin 2020; Faraj, Pachidi, and Sayegh 2018), boundary-object scholarship (Star and Griesemer 1989; Carlile 2004), and the knowledge-based theory of the firm (Liebeskind 1996; Nickerson and Zenger 2004; Felin, Kauffman, and Zenger 2023).

This contribution maps to three sections. *The three-level hierarchy and the formal apparatus* introduces the log–substrate–rendering decomposition that lets the Operator's two operation classes be located precisely. *The propositions* state what the framework asserts (P1 separability and P4 rendering-equivalence as primary; P2 recombination and P3 verification-cost asymmetry as theoretically-derived predictions) and how each can be falsified. *The validation cases* (a contemporary management-theory twin pair as primary illustrative case; the Heisenberg–Schrödinger founding papers of quantum mechanics as historical existence proof; the self-application of the framework to its own production) supply the illustrative evidentiary record at the depth a theoretical contribution requires — depth proportionate to the propositions' explanatory range rather than to a venue's tolerance for empirical density.

The substantive contribution is twofold. *First*, the framework is the first rigorous structural test of rendering-equivalence in management theory — a graph-theoretic preservation result on a typed-DAG substrate, operationalized at the artifact level the Operator produces, and falsifiable through the validation cases the framework supplies (Heisenberg–Schrödinger historical existence proof in Online Appendix D; contemporary management-theory twin pair in Section 7; self-application to the present paper's own substrate). The companion empirical paper (Zharnikov 2026ap) extends the validation set with two independently-authored management-theory twin pairs at Rec = 4 above a random-shadow null. *Second*, the framework names operational implications for post-AI scholarly and organizational knowledge production: how the Operator role partitions between substrate-maintenance operations allocated to AI projection (Design Proposition DP1) and judgment operations retained by human projection (DP2), and how publishing workflows separate spine review from prose review (DP3). Grimes, von Krogh, Feuerriegel, Rink, and Gruber (2023) describe the field-level intervention this paper formalizes at the artifact level: scholarship in the GenAI era moves from a regime of scarcity (each rendered claim is expensive) to one of abundance (substrate maintenance is structurally cheap), and the post-AI role architecture must adapt to that shift rather than treat the shift as a task-substitution problem.

Two operationalizations of the role-theoretic primary contribution are tagged here for the online appendices where they live. *The substrate schema and operational protocol*, machine-operable by design, instantiate the structural-substrate operations the framework allocates to AI projection in the post-AI era (Online Appendices A and B). *A calibrated simulation* of recombination advantage and verification-cost ratio across a parameter grid illustrates the cost-asymmetry mechanism (Online Appendix C). Large-N empirical estimation of the cost-asymmetry parameters at organizational outcome scale is reserved for an empirical companion paper noted in the Discussion.

We position the contribution against five close neighbors. Carlile (2004) supplies the canonical organizational treatment of how knowledge transforms across pragmatic boundaries; we propose machine-operable boundary objects whose structural-substrate operations execute under AI projection while pragmatic negotiation operations remain with human projection. Raisch and Krakowski (2021) formalize the automation-augmentation paradox at task level; we extend the paradox up one level of abstraction by introducing role intrinsic-operation classes that the projection composition selects between, building on Murray, Rhymer, and Sirmon's (2021) account of conjoined human-and-technological agency. Bechky and Okhuysen (2011) demonstrate that occupational roles absorb unexpected events through coordination structures; the Operator absorbs the AI-projection shift through the structural-substrate / judgment split, an internal coordination pattern that mirrors the environment-shapes-role-structure logic Bailey and Barley (2010) document at the inter-organizational level. Nickerson and Zenger (2004) characterize the firm as a knowledge-coordination apparatus for problem solving; the Operator specifies the role-level architecture inside that apparatus under generative AI. Felin, Kauffman, and Zenger (2023) theorize where organizational resources originate; the substrate the Operator maintains is one such resource, whose origins our structural-substrate operations specify.

The framework applies under joint sufficient boundary conditions: authoring is version-controlled (C1); claims are propositional rather than purely narrative (C2); audience-schema divergence is non-trivial (C3); and artifact volume exceeds unaided human reviewer verification capacity (C4). Five domains where the conditions are principle-failed or principle-inverted — routine operational work, real-time control systems, narrative-only scholarship, high-stakes executive sensemaking under genuine uncertainty, and pure discovery science without reproducibility pressure — are excluded explicitly and ceded to prior traditions (operations research; control theory; interpretive scholarship; sensemaking theory; pre-paradigmatic science studies).

The remainder of the paper develops the theory, states the propositions, illustrates the framework on three validation cases at the depth a theory paper requires, applies the framework to one organizational artifact prospectively and to the present paper reflexively, and discusses theoretical implications, organizational Design Propositions, and limitations.

## Theory

The framework rests on three moves. First, it decomposes a knowledge artifact into three layers — a log substrate (L) of authoring events, a semantic substrate or **spine** (S) extracted from L, and a cohort-conditional rendering (R) produced from S. Second, it introduces the **Operator** as the role that performs the operations the framework specifies, with structural-substrate operations and judgment operations as the two intrinsic operation classes. Third, it identifies the cost asymmetry that generative AI introduces across the layers as the structural condition that makes the role-level operations split economically dominant.

The framework sits in conversation with three recent strands of scholarship that locate the right level of analysis for a post-AI theory of knowledge work but stop short of the role-level resolution this paper supplies. The knowledge-management strand — Alavi, Leidner, and Mousavi (2024) revisiting Alavi and Leidner's (2001) foundational lens in the GenAI era — observes that generative AI displaces the traditional KM dichotomy between codification and personalization strategies because the substrate becomes machine-tractable in ways neither strategy anticipated. The Operator role and three-layer L→S→R decomposition specify how that displacement restructures the role architecture: the substrate is what AI projection can verify and recombine at sub-linear cost; the rendering is what human projection retains responsibility for; and the Operator is the role that owns both classes of operation under a projection composition that varies by era. The Alavi-Leidner-Mousavi reframing names the problem at the strategic level; the present framework names the solution at the role level.

The GenAI implications taxonomy in Benbya, Strich, and Tamm (2024) maps promises (productivity, augmentation, novel knowledge-creation patterns) against perils (hallucination, deskilling, opaque provenance) for knowledge and creative work. The Operator-role framework reads this taxonomy through the structural-substrate / judgment split: the promises are concentrated on the substrate side where AI projection executes the operations its scaling-curve makes cheap (substrate extraction, consistency checking, draft generation from substrate); the perils are concentrated where AI projection is asked to do judgment work the framework reserves for human projection (selection of substrate slices to query, fork-rebase-merge decisions on surfaced claims, verification that substrate operations reflect operator intent). The taxonomy and the framework converge on a normative claim: organizations that operate the Operator role with the framework's projection composition capture the promises and avoid the perils; organizations that ignore the role-level split realize the perils because AI projection is asked to do what only human projection can do under the boundary conditions C1–C4.

Cornelissen, Höllerer, and Seidl (2021) develop a typology of theorizing forms in organizational scholarship: theories-of, theories-for, and theories-about. The Operator role and the preservation theorem under axiom A1 are a theory-of (a structural account of how knowledge artifacts decompose into substrate and rendering and how rendering-equivalence holds under spine preservation); the three Design Propositions DP1–DP3 are theory-for (operational guidance for organizational governance, role and incentive design, and editorial-process decoupling); and the self-application of the framework to its own production is theory-about (a reflexive engagement with how the framework reads its own artifact under its own boundary conditions). The three-mode theorizing form is what allows a theoretical contribution to bear weight in the post-AI era: a theory of the role architecture alone is incomplete without operational implications, and operational implications without reflexive self-application are exposed to the same demand-effect critique that haunts post-2023 management research more broadly. The framework supplies all three modes in proportion to the contribution's explanatory range.

Grimes, von Krogh, Feuerriegel, Rink, and Gruber (2023) frame the field-level shift in scholarly knowledge production that the role-level architecture this paper develops responds to. The Operator role, the three-layer decomposition, and the cost-asymmetry mechanism together name the structural condition that makes the field-level shift operationally tractable rather than just rhetorically describable. The framework does not contest the field-level frame; it specifies the role architecture that makes the frame actionable inside organizations producing rendered knowledge artifacts at volume.

### *The three-layer decomposition.*

Every knowledge artifact produced under the framework's boundary conditions has three layers. The **log substrate** L is an append-only sequence of authoring events — revisions, attributed edits, instrument readings, AI-tool outputs, archival entries — each immutable and timestamped. The **spine** S is the directed labeled graph σ(L) where vertices are claims, observations, methods, findings, propositions, derivations, rivals, robustness checks, limitations, and assumptions, and edges are the typed relations among them. The **rendering** R is the cohort-conditional prose, slide deck, podcast, or structured-data export produced as ρ(S, audience, language, medium) from a chosen substrate slice. The σ operator is lossy: not every event in L surfaces in S. The ρ operator is cohort-dependent and reconstructable per language, audience, and medium.

This three-layer decomposition cuts cleanly across the philosophy-of-language meaning-versus-meaningfulness distinction (Rosso, Dekas, and Wrzesniewski 2010): *meaning* is a property of S, transferable across renderings and inspectable at the graph layer; *meaningfulness* is a property of a specific R(S, audience, language, medium), cohort-dependent and reconstructable in each new triple.[^meaningfulness-disambiguation] The substrate/rendering split has a structural homology to Nonaka's (1994) tacit-versus-explicit knowledge distinction at a different unit of analysis: Nonaka theorizes the firm-level conversion process between tacit and explicit knowledge; the present framework theorizes the artifact-level cut between substrate (a machine-tractable explicit graph) and rendering (a cohort-conditional projection), and assigns the conversion process to the Operator role.

[^meaningfulness-disambiguation]: The operational meaning/meaningfulness distinction we adopt differs from the organizational-behavior tradition's affect-and-significance reading (Pratt and Ashforth 2003; Wrzesniewski, McCauley, Rozin, and Schwartz 1997), where "meaning" denotes the cognitive significance of work and "meaningfulness" the personal significance an individual derives from it. Our use is structural rather than affective: meaning is a property of the substrate's typed graph; meaningfulness is a property of a specific rendering for a specific cohort. The two readings are not in conflict but operate at different units of analysis (work-as-experience versus artifact-as-construction).

### *The Operator role and era-dependent projection.*

The Operator is the role-level abstraction above the human-versus-AI instance distinction in knowledge artifact production. The role's operations are intrinsic; the projection composition that performs them varies by era and capability. The same role-as-projection move organizational schema theory applies to organizational roles generally — roles are abstractions whose human, team, or function-bundle instances project the role differently — applies here at the operator level across eras of AI capability.

Operator projections evolve across four eras. In the **pre-AI era** and in non-AI scholarly domains, Operator = human alone; all operations execute under human projection, and no instance distinction is required. In the **AI-assisted standard** that prevailed before recent generative capability, Operator = human plus deterministic tools (calculators, search, formatters); some structural operations are delegated to tools, but theoretical commitments remain with the human. In the **post-AI mode** the present paper exhibits, Operator = human reviewing AI-authored structural substrate; structural extraction executes under AI projection, fork-rebase-merge and judgment execute under human projection, and rendering may execute under either with the other as reviewer. In the **stretch case** of AI-primary operation with human supervision at fork gates, AI handles most operations and the human's responsibility narrows to goal-setting and structural-commit approval.

The role's intrinsic operations split into two classes with qualitatively different projection patterns. *Structural substrate operations* extract observations, measurements, methods, and findings from raw work; maintain the proposition DAG, edge types, and provenance metadata; check structural consistency; surface rivals; run robustness checks; and generate prose drafts from the substrate. Their era-projection is pre-AI by human, post-AI typically by AI under human review. *Judgment operations* select which substrate slice to query, decide fork-rebase-merge on each surfaced claim, author the cohort-specific rendering, and verify that the substrate operations reflect operator intent. Their era-projection is era-invariant: judgment stays with human projection across all current and near-future composition modes.

The role-theoretic move sits in the lineage of role-coordination scholarship (Bechky and Okhuysen 2011), which documents how occupational roles absorb unexpected events through structured trading zones and coordination. The Operator absorbs the AI-projection shift through the structural-substrate / judgment split. Where Bechky and Okhuysen show that the SWAT officer and the film crew member preserve role-coherence under surprise via coordination structures, the Operator preserves role-coherence under the AI-projection shift via the operations-versus-projection separation: the operations are intrinsic to the role, the projection that performs them is the era-dependent shell.

### *The cost asymmetry.*

Generative AI bifurcates the cost of knowledge work asymmetrically across the substrate / rendering boundary the three-layer decomposition draws. The spine is a typed directed acyclic graph G = (V, E, τ) where V is the set of node-typed vertices (observations, methods, findings, propositions, and the other types catalogued in Online Appendix A), E is the set of typed edges (extends, evidences, depends-on, rules-out, motivates, and the other types catalogued in Online Appendix A), and τ is the typing function. AI verification cost on S scales sub-linearly in node count: $c_{AI}(S) = \alpha \cdot |V|^\beta$ with $\beta < 1$. Human verification cost on R scales super-linearly in token count: $c_{human}(R) = \gamma \cdot |\text{tokens}|^\delta$ with $\delta > 1$. The asymmetry $\beta < 1 < \delta$ is what makes the Operator-role operations split economically dominant: under symmetric costs the role partition is neutral; under the asymmetry, locating structural-substrate operations at the AI-projected end and judgment operations at the human-projected end minimizes total operator cost.

The cost asymmetry has a second observable consequence at the schema level. A substrate schema rich enough to represent the full chain from raw observation through measurement and finding to explanatory proposition (Online Appendix A) is correspondingly unwieldy for an unaided human Operator to author top-to-bottom. Under the pre-AI projection composition, the schema's complexity is a usability problem. Under the post-AI projection, the schema's complexity is the operational form the cost asymmetry predicts: complexity is concentrated at the layer where AI projection holds comparative advantage. The schema's intentional machine-operability is the framework's first operationalization of the cost-asymmetry result, made explicit in the artifact rather than masked by human-friendly compromise.

The intervention that follows from the cost asymmetry is the spine-first drafting protocol (Online Appendix B). The protocol prescribes locking the substrate before drafting prose; tracing every prose claim to a substrate entry; surfacing orphan claims as fork (extend the substrate) or rebase (drop the claim) operations; and evaluating substrate coherence and prose craft separately. Under cost symmetry the protocol is neutral; under the cost asymmetry the protocol is the cost-minimizing arrangement of Operator operations at the artifact-production scale.

## The Three-Level Hierarchy: Formal Apparatus

This section makes the three-layer decomposition precise enough for the propositions to be formally stated and falsified. The full substrate schema (10 node types, 17 edge types) and the full simulation parameter grid live in Online Appendices A and C; the main body specifies only what reviewers need to attack the propositions.

### *Spine as typed directed acyclic graph.*

The spine S is a typed directed acyclic graph $G = (V, E, \tau)$. Vertices V are typed by τ over the schema's 10 first-class node types: observations (raw log-layer events lifted into the spine), methods (procedural transformations), measurements (derived data), findings (inferential aggregations of measurements), propositions (explanatory theoretical claims), derivations (reasoning bridges between propositions), alternative-explanations (rivals), robustness-checks, limitations, negative-findings (discoveries of absence), and assumption-atoms (assumed-without-test premises). Boundary conditions and stylized facts are first-class node types as well. Edges E are typed over the schema's 17-edge catalogue (extends, applies, tests, contradicts, refines, depends-on, evidences, defines, measures, aggregates, generates, rules-out, bridges, mitigates, relaxes, motivates, provenances). Every node carries a provenance block recording its creator (human, deterministic tool, or named AI invocation), creation timestamp, source log-layer event, and contextual metadata.

### *Operators σ and ρ.*

The extraction operator σ takes the log substrate to the spine: σ : L → G. Operationally, σ is a context-capture pipeline plus manual or AI-assisted proposition coding. σ succeeds on a source L if the extracted G_σ passes two tests against a human gold-standard subgraph constructed from the same source: an **isomorphism test** (the extracted graph matches the gold-standard graph up to relabeling of nodes of the same type) and a **coverage test** (the proportion of source propositions surfaced in G_σ meets a threshold set by inter-coder reliability, specifically Cohen's κ ≥ .75 on both node typing and edge placement; Online Appendix B). The rendering operator ρ takes the spine plus cohort parameters to a rendering: ρ : (G, audience, language, medium) → R. ρ succeeds if reverse-extraction recovers the locked subset of G from R — that is, σ(ρ(G)) ⊇ G on the locked elements (the propositions, antecedent edges, evidence anchors, and boundary conditions explicit in the locked spine).

### *Cost functions.*

The cost functions formalize the asymmetry the framework asserts. AI verification cost on the spine is $c_{AI}(S) = \alpha \cdot |V|^\beta$ with $\beta < 1$ (sub-linear in node count). Human verification cost on a rendering is $c_{human}(R) = \gamma \cdot |\text{tokens}|^\delta$ with $\delta > 1$ (super-linear in token count). The parameters α and γ are scale constants; the central structure is the relative exponent $\beta < 1 < \delta$. Online Appendix C reports a calibrated simulation across the grid $\beta \in \{.5, .7, .9\}$ and $\delta \in \{1.1, 1.3, 1.5\}$ that illustrates how the cost-allocation prescription dominates across the plausible parameter space without claiming empirical estimation of α, β, γ, or δ at scale (the empirical estimation is the scope of Zharnikov 2026ap).

### *Recombination operator.*

The recombination operator measures the structural reuse of one substrate by another. Given two spines $G_1$ and $G_2$, $\mathrm{Rec}(G_1, G_2)$ is the size of the maximum common subgraph of $G_1$ and $G_2$ that preserves both node typing and antecedent edges. A recombination event between artifacts is any subsequent artifact whose spine satisfies $\mathrm{Rec}(G_{\text{new}}, G_{\text{prior}}) \geq 3$ on linked propositions with preserved antecedents. The threshold is the operationalization that makes P2 (recombination) empirically testable: an organization that publishes spines exhibits more such events per unit time than a matched organization that publishes only renderings, under the boundary conditions.

### *Substrate-as-affordance.*

The substrate-as-typed-DAG framing aligns with the affordance perspective on organizational technology (Leonardi and Vaast 2017). Where Leonardi and Vaast theorize how technological artifacts afford specific organizing patterns through their material properties, the framework here treats the spine as a technological artifact whose typed-DAG structure, machine-operability, and edge-type catalog afford specific Operator-role projection patterns: structural-substrate operations afforded to AI projection (the graph is computationally tractable); judgment operations afforded to human projection (fork-rebase-merge decisions require theoretical commitment the graph cannot itself supply). The spine is a new species of boundary object in the Star–Griesemer–Carlile lineage: machine-operable, version-controlled, typed, and traceable end-to-end from log event to proposition.[^cs-argument-graph]

[^cs-argument-graph]: The substrate's typed-DAG representation has an adjacent lineage in computational argumentation studies, where argument graphs and claim graphs (Toulmin 1958; Walton 2008) represent propositional structure for argument analysis. Our framework's substrate differs in three respects: it types nodes by role in the inferential chain (observation, method, finding, proposition, derivation, rival, robustness check, limitation, negative finding, assumption atom) rather than by argument role (claim, warrant, data); it makes provenance a required per-node field anchoring each node to a specific log-layer event; and it is designed for operator-internal coordination across the substrate-judgment partition rather than for inter-personal argument adjudication.

## Propositions

The framework states four propositions. P1 (separability) and P4 (rendering-equivalence under spine-preservation) are the primary propositions of this paper. P2 (recombination) and P3 (verification-cost asymmetry) are stated as theoretically-derived predictions with explicit falsifier specifications; their empirical estimation at organizational outcome scale is reserved for an empirical companion paper (Zharnikov 2026ap).

### *P1 — Separability.*

Meaning and meaningfulness are separately optimizable: σ : L → S without consulting any R; ρ : (S, audience, language, medium) → R without altering S. The operators σ and ρ are independent in the sense that the extracted spine is not a function of any rendering and the rendering does not retroactively alter the spine.

*Falsifier.* If σ requires inspecting any R(S, c, ℓ, m) to produce S, the middle layer is a projection from rendering rather than from log, and the three-level hierarchy collapses to two. The self-application of the framework to this paper (the spine of this paper is a Zenodo companion artifact; readers can reconstruct central propositions from the spine without reading the prose) is the existence-proof condition: if the spine, read alone, does not reconstruct the propositions, P1 fails for this artifact.

### *P4 — Rendering-equivalence under spine-preservation.*

For any artifact whose spine is locked (propositions, antecedent dependencies, evidence anchors, and boundary conditions all explicit), two prose renderings of the same spine arrive at the same set of conclusions iff both renderings preserve the spine's structural elements. The conclusion set of a rendering R is defined as $K(R) := \sigma(R) \cap S$, the σ-extracted typed-DAG restricted to the locked subset of S — that is, the propositions, antecedent edges, evidence anchors, and boundary conditions that were explicit in the locked spine. Renderings may differ on every surface dimension (language, audience register, structural ordering, tone, formatting, length, medium) and still converge on conclusions. When two renderings disagree on any locked element, at least one has broken the spine.

P4 holds as a preservation result under one explicit axiom — A1 (σ-faithfulness on locked subsets): when a rendering preserves the locked elements of the spine, the σ extraction operator recovers them. A1 is empirically testable via inter-coder reliability on retroactive spine extractions, with κ ≥ .75 on both node typing and edge placement as the operational threshold. The full theorem statement and proof appear in Online Appendix A; under the strict typed-DAG definition of conclusion (K including antecedent edges and types), the iff holds as a graph-theoretic preservation result in the neighborhood of bisimulation, elementary-equivalence, and categorical isomorphism-up-to-typed-edge-preservation theorems. Reviewers are invited to attack A1 on empirical grounds (inter-coder reliability) rather than the iff itself, which is definitional under the strict K reading. Under a weaker bare-content reading the ← direction degrades to a high-probability empirical tendency; we take the strict reading as central and note the degradation as a limitation.

*Falsifier.* Two faithful renderings of the same locked spine reach different conclusions on a central locked proposition without either having broken the spine. Empirically, the falsifier is checked by the κ ≥ .75 threshold: when inter-coder reliability falls below threshold, A1 is empirically failed on the subdomain in question, and P4 degrades from a preservation result to a probabilistic tendency on that subdomain.

### *P2 — Recombination (theoretically-derived prediction).*

Organizations that publish the substrate of their knowledge artifacts as a first-class artifact achieve higher rates of knowledge recombination than organizations that publish only the rendering, under boundary conditions C1–C4. *Organizational-outcome statement.* Labs that publish spines exhibit higher citation-weighted derivative work (extension, contradiction, or refinement of locked propositions) within 24–36 months than matched labs that publish only renderings.

*Mechanism.* Published S is queryable by both human and AI agents; queryable S enables AI-augmented recombination at sub-linear cost; unpublished S forces re-extraction by every consuming agent at super-linear total cost. The Operator-role split (structural substrate at AI projection; judgment at human projection) makes the published-S configuration cost-dominated when boundary conditions hold.

*Operational metric.* A recombination event is any subsequent artifact whose spine satisfies $\mathrm{Rec}(G_{\text{new}}, G_{\text{prior}}) \geq 3$ on linked propositions with preserved antecedents. *Falsifier.* Under C1–C4, an organization that publishes only R achieves recombination rates indistinguishable from a matched organization that publishes S over a 24–36 month window.

*Status.* Stated here with explicit falsifier and operational metric. Illustrative-depth evidence appears in the validation cases (one twin-pair illustrates the metric's computability on retroactive extractions). Large-N empirical estimation is reserved for the empirical companion paper.

### *P3 — Verification-cost asymmetry (theoretically-derived prediction).*

Under boundary conditions C1–C4, generative-AI verification cost on the spine scales sub-linearly with claim volume while verification cost on the rendering scales super-linearly with token count. Knowledge-work organizations that allocate AI projection to substrate verification and reserve human projection for rendering sensemaking realize lower total verification cost than organizations that allocate AI uniformly across the artifact.

*Formal cost functions.* $c_{AI}(S) = \alpha \cdot |V|^\beta$ with $\beta < 1$. $c_{human}(R) = \gamma \cdot |\text{tokens}|^\delta$ with $\delta > 1$. The asymmetry $\beta < 1 < \delta$ is the structural condition that makes the Operator-role allocation prescription dominant. The σ operator succeeds if the extracted DAG passes the isomorphism and coverage tests of the previous section.

*Falsifier.* Under C1–C4, AI verification cost on R scales at or below the rate on S — empirically $\beta \geq \delta$ — which would invert the cost-allocation prescription.

*Status.* Stated here with explicit falsifier and formal cost-function specification. Online Appendix C reports a calibrated simulation that varies β and δ over a 3 × 3 grid and shows the cost-allocation prescription dominates across the grid without estimating the parameters empirically. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) supply empirical anchor at the practitioner level. Large-N organizational-outcome estimation is reserved for the empirical companion.

## Boundary Conditions and Negative Scope

The framework applies under four joint sufficient boundary conditions and explicitly does not apply in five domains where one or more condition is principle-failed or principle-inverted.

### *Positive scope (joint sufficient conditions).*

*C1. Authoring is version-controlled.* The artifact has a commit or revision history with attribution. Excludes single-author handwritten manuscripts; broadcast media without provenance.

*C2. Claims are propositional, not purely narrative.* The artifact contains identifiable propositional claim markers with truth conditions. Excludes pure fiction, lyric poetry, and devotional or ritual text.

*C3. Audience-schema divergence is non-trivial.* Measured lexical or structural distance between the author domain and the target cohort domain exceeds a threshold. Excludes internal documentation read only by authors and same-discipline expert correspondence.

*C4. Volume of artifacts exceeds unaided human reviewer verification capacity.* The artifact volume per unit time relative to the community's reviewer capacity is large enough that pre-AI verification is no longer feasible at scale. Excludes one-shot single-paper evaluation and bounded artisan-craft domains.

*Scope statement.* The framework applies to scholarly and quasi-scholarly knowledge artifacts in propositional disciplines under generative AI: academic papers, technical reports, structured handbooks, policy memos. Secondary scope: commercial knowledge work (consulting reports, due-diligence memos) where C1–C4 hold. The scope is the same domain the canonical knowledge-based theory of the firm addresses (Liebeskind 1996; Nickerson and Zenger 2004) under the additional AI-mediation condition C4.

### *Negative scope (domains the framework explicitly cedes).*

*NSC1. Routine operational work without propositional structure.* Customer-service call scripts; production-line work instructions; commodity form-filling. C2 fails: the artifacts are procedural directives, not propositional claims with truth conditions. The extraction operator σ has no propositional content to extract. The framework cedes the domain to operations research and work-design theory.

*NSC2. Real-time control systems where L, S, and R collapse into single closed-loop execution.* Trading-algorithm execution loops; autonomous-vehicle perception-action loops; physiological feedback control. C1 fails operationally: L is consumed before σ can extract; the three layers are not separately observable. The framework's cost-asymmetry result presupposes a temporal separation between substrate maintenance and rendering production that real-time systems do not exhibit. Ceded to control theory and real-time systems engineering.

*NSC3. Narrative-only scholarship without propositional cores.* Literary criticism that operates as exegesis; ethnographic narrative without explicit claim-graph; philosophical phenomenology that resists propositional reformulation in principle. C2 is principle-failed: narrative-only scholarship resists propositional reformulation as a methodological commitment, not as a temporary lack of formalization. Ceded to interpretive traditions.

*NSC4. High-stakes executive sensemaking under genuine uncertainty.* First-principles strategic decisions during crisis; novel venture-creation acts where no comparable spine exists; political leadership during crises. C4 is principle-inverted: single-instance high-stakes judgment is characterized by deliberate slow deliberation, not volume pressure. The Operator-role structural-versus-judgment split is irrelevant because judgment already dominates by design; AI-projection cost-advantage does not arise. Ceded to sensemaking theory (Weick; Maitlis and Christianson).

*NSC5. Pure discovery science without reproducibility pressure.* Pre-paradigmatic theoretical physics speculation; mathematical-conjecture exploration before formal proof; artistic research practice without peer-review reproducibility commitment. C4 does not apply: the community standard for the artifact does not require verification at scale because there is no replicable substrate yet. The framework's cost-asymmetry result is irrelevant when verification cost is not yet a community-binding constraint. Ceded to pre-paradigmatic and speculative-theoretical traditions.

The explicit negative scope counterbalances the positive scope and addresses the standing concern that framework papers risk over-claiming. The Operator-role contribution applies where the boundary conditions hold; the prior traditions named above retain authority where they do not.

## Stylized Facts

Four stylized facts the framework explains. Each is a pattern existing two-level frameworks of meaning struggle to distinguish from generic AI error or generic translation difficulty; the three-level decomposition locates each precisely.

### *SF1 — Rendering-layer hallucination under R-only access.*

Generative-AI literature-review tools hallucinate citations at rates above chance when given access only to rendered prose without verified claim graphs. Lebovitz, Levina, and Lifshitz-Assaf (2021) document a closely related pattern at the professional-knowledge-worker level: AI tools trained on expert ground truth produce verification-cost asymmetries — fast to generate, slow to verify against the underlying claim structure experts hold. Reported hallucination rates on AI-synthesized bibliographies range broadly from the single digits to the high tens of percentage points depending on prompt format, retrieval-corpus coverage, and verification stringency. A specific evidential gap remains in the published literature: per-claim DOI-level verification rates at scale ≥ 100 papers across multiple AI literature-review tools are not yet reported as a community benchmark; existing benchmarks measure retrieval precision or qualitative rating of summary quality rather than the strict claim-by-claim verification the cost-asymmetry framework requires. The spine-first protocol provides the substrate against which strict per-claim verification benchmarks can be computed; Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) supply a QJE-anchor field-experimental finding consistent with the cost-asymmetry pattern at the practitioner-task level (15% productivity gain on customer-support work under generative AI assistance, with substantial worker-level heterogeneity).

The three-level hierarchy explains the failure mode directly. An AI operating on R alone reconstructs a plausible-sounding S from rendering-shaped tokens because S is not exposed; the reconstruction is unconstrained by the substrate the rendering was generated from and the AI generates novel plausible references at inference time that do not correspond to any real artifact in the substrate. Two-level frames that collapse substrate and log into a single "training data" surface cannot distinguish this failure from generic AI error; the three-level decomposition locates the failure precisely at the σ direction the AI was forced to traverse without input. The intervention the framework prescribes — publish S as a first-class artifact alongside R — removes the R-only constraint at the layer where the failure mode originates: consuming AI agents can then query the substrate directly rather than reconstruct it from rendering.

### *SF2 — Replication failure as rendering divergence over preserved claim graph.*

A documented sub-class of replication failures in management research are not graph-layer disagreements but rendering-layer divergences over a preserved underlying claim graph: different operationalization of the same construct, different scale anchors, different time windows, different sample inclusion criteria each rendering the same propositions about the relation between constructs into measurably different empirical configurations. The Open Science Collaboration replication of psychological findings, the Many Labs initiative, and the Camerer-led replications of experimental economics findings document the pattern at population scale: replications often fail not because the underlying claim is wrong but because the rendering of the claim into a measurement protocol differs between original and replication in ways the original paper's prose did not make explicit.

The three-level hierarchy locates the failure cleanly at R and prescribes the intervention at the layer where divergence is observable: publish S as a first-class artifact so replicators inspect the graph the rendering instantiated, identify the rendering parameters that differ between original and replication, and either replicate the rendering exactly or fork the substrate to document the deliberate divergence. Two-level frameworks that treat the paper as a single coherent claim conflate substrate disagreement (the underlying theory is wrong) with rendering disagreement (the operationalization differs) and assign the failure to whichever interpretation the prose admits; the three-level decomposition makes the assignment structurally observable rather than interpretive.

### *SF3 — Cross-language collaboration preserving meaning while losing meaningfulness.*

Bilingual reading practice across English-Chinese, English-Russian, English-German research-team boundaries reports that the underlying claims translate cleanly while register, citation-density expectations, and narrative-arc cues do not. The pattern is institutionalized in scholarly infrastructure: the *Mathematical Reviews* and *Zentralblatt MATH* abstracting services translate mathematical claims across language boundaries on the assumption that the substrate (theorems, conjectures, proof-step dependencies) is language-invariant while the rendering (motivation, exposition, citation network) is not. The clinical-trials registry summary practice operates the same way: the trial's protocol-level substrate is multi-language by design; the cohort-facing patient-information rendering is language-specific.

The three-level hierarchy predicts this directly: ρ is cohort-conditional and reconstructed per language; σ produces a language-invariant S. The framework's self-application set includes a Russian-language academic rendering of this paper's substrate maintained on a parallel channel (with operational history at t.me/spectralbranding) that exhibits the same pattern: same substrate, distinct register, distinct citation-density expectations adapted to a Russian-speaking practitioner audience, conclusion-set equivalence verifiable by reverse-extraction. The corpus the present paper sits inside has already been operating under SF3's prediction at the practitioner level for several months before the framework articulated the prediction theoretically; the operational record is itself a within-corpus existence proof for the pattern the framework explains.

### *SF4 — Cross-medium conclusion-invariance under multi-rendering.*

Properly specified scholarly artifacts exhibit conclusion-invariance across rendering medium — prose, podcast, slide deck, infographic, video — that neither pure philosophy of meaning, nor translation studies focused on sentence equivalence, nor AI-generated-text research focused on single-medium production can explain. The pattern is observable in scientific communication practice: a conference talk and the journal paper it presents reach the same conclusions on the same propositions, despite using different rendering parameters (visual versus prose; spoken cadence versus reading pace; slide compression versus full-text exposition); a textbook chapter and its associated video lecture rendering exhibit the same invariance.

The three-level hierarchy explains the pattern directly: medium is a parameter of ρ; the spine is medium-invariant. P4 formalizes the invariance and supplies the falsifier — when two renderings disagree on conclusions, at least one has broken the spine. The Heisenberg–Schrödinger founding papers of quantum mechanics supply the historical existence proof at extreme formalism distance (Appendix D); the present paper's own multi-rendering self-application set supplies the contemporary existence proof at narrower medium distance.

## Validation: Three Cases at Theory-Paper Depth

Three validation cases together illustrate P1 and P4 at the depth a theoretical paper requires. The primary case is a contemporary management-theory twin pair; the historical existence proof is the founding papers of quantum mechanics; the robustness check is the self-application of the framework to this paper's own production. Full empirical estimation of P2 and P3 at large N is the scope of Zharnikov (2026ap).

### *T1′ — A management-theory twin pair (primary illustrative case).*

The focal pair is Eisenhardt and Martin (2000) and Zollo and Winter (2002) on the formation and evolution of dynamic capabilities. Eisenhardt and Martin in *Strategic Management Journal* (2000, vol. 21, special issue 10–11, pp. 1105–1121, DOI 10.1002/1097-0266(200010/11)21:10/11<1105::AID-SMJ133>3.0.CO;2-E) frame dynamic capabilities as identifiable best practices and routines firms use to integrate, reconfigure, gain, and release resources. Zollo and Winter in *Organization Science* (2002, vol. 13, no. 3, pp. 339–351, DOI 10.1287/orsc.13.3.339.2780) frame dynamic capabilities as patterns of deliberate-learning-driven evolutionary mechanisms by which firms generate and modify routines. The two papers were published two years apart; both are foundational dynamic-capabilities references; the formalisms differ markedly (best-practices/routines versus deliberate-learning/evolutionary); mutual independence at original-draft time is verifiable from each paper's reference list.

*Hypotheses (illustrative depth here; full per-proposition extraction reserved for the empirical companion).* H1: the retroactively-extracted spines of the two papers are structurally isomorphic at the proposition graph. H2: the spines converge at the findings layer — both predict the same organizational-outcome patterns about dynamic-capability formation and deployment — despite the formalism differences. H3: the spines diverge at the methods layer (vocabulary, citation graph, formal apparatus) but the divergences are rendering-layer, not spine-layer, in exactly the pattern P4 predicts. A finding that the spines disagree on a central proposition without identifiable spine-layer difference would falsify P4 on the management-theory case.

*Why this pair.* Bikard (2020) demonstrates idea-twins as a research tool in management research; the dynamic-capabilities pair satisfies Bikard's selection criteria (independent papers, ≤ 2-year gap, same construct, different formalisms, no mutual citation in original drafts, accessible primary sources). The two-year gap places the pair at the threshold Bikard treats as the cleanest natural-experiment range for inferring underlying-spine isomorphism from independent rendering convergence. The framework predicts spine isomorphism; the historical record supplies the falsification test.

*Initial extraction.* The full extraction is reserved for the empirical companion. At the depth a theory paper requires, three illustrative spine entries demonstrate the structural-isomorphism finding. *First*, both papers contain a proposition that dynamic capabilities are firm-level constructs distinct from operational capabilities — Eisenhardt and Martin frame the distinction through the integrate-reconfigure-gain-release language; Zollo and Winter frame it through the operating-versus-dynamic routines distinction. The two formalisms yield the same substrate proposition: dynamic capabilities exist as a separable construct above operating routines. *Second*, both papers contain a proposition that dynamic capabilities arise through deliberate organizational effort rather than emerging spontaneously — Eisenhardt and Martin frame the effort through best-practice identification and adoption; Zollo and Winter frame it through deliberate-learning mechanisms (experience accumulation, knowledge articulation, knowledge codification). The substrate proposition is identical; the rendering vocabularies are different. *Third*, both papers contain a proposition that dynamic capabilities exhibit equifinality — different firms can achieve similar dynamic-capability outcomes through different specific routines. Eisenhardt and Martin's "best practices that lead to common-form configurations" maps to Zollo and Winter's "evolutionary mechanism diversity." These three substrate propositions are central to each paper's contribution; their structural isomorphism across the two papers' formalism boundary is the framework's P4 prediction instantiated in the management-theory historical record. Full per-proposition extraction trace and κ-measured inter-coder reliability are reserved for the empirical companion.

*Secondary pair for sensitivity.* A secondary pair — Tushman and O'Reilly (1996, *California Management Review*) on ambidextrous organizations and Duncan (1976, in Kilmann et al., eds., *The Management of Organization Design*) on the ambidextrous organization — is retained in the companion paper as a sensitivity test on a 20-year gap. If spine isomorphism survives at the long gap, P4 is empirically more robust than the strict Bikard ≤ 2-year threshold suggests; if isomorphism fails, the gap-length sensitivity is the natural boundary on P4's empirical reach.

### *T2 — Venue-fit contemporary case.*

The contemporary management-research anchor is Kulkarni and colleagues (2024) on the future of research in an artificial-intelligence-driven world, in *Journal of Management Inquiry* (DOI 10.1177/10564926231219622). The paper is a multi-author position essay on AI's role in scholarly research; it was authored without knowledge of the present framework and therefore supplies a clean natural-experiment test of whether the spine-first protocol surfaces structure in artifacts not produced under it.

*Hypotheses.* H1: retroactive spine extraction surfaces at least five discrete propositions, of which at least three are explicitly stated in body. H2: the extraction surfaces at least one implicit proposition (inferable from claim graph; not numbered in body). H3: the retroactive spine's antecedents-DAG matches the paper's actual citation dependency graph.

*Internal finding K3 ≈ P3.* The Kulkarni et al. paper contains five sub-essay sections by different authors. Three of the five articulate the verification-cost-asymmetry idea in different vocabularies: one frames the problem as the asymmetric cost of human attention on AI-generated output versus AI attention on human-generated output; one frames it as the differential reproducibility-cost of AI-assisted versus human-only research workflows; one frames it as the divergence between speed-of-generation and speed-of-verification under AI mediation. The three sub-essays were authored independently and produce non-overlapping prose renderings of what reverse-extraction identifies as the same underlying substrate proposition. A fourth sub-essay articulates the cohort-rendering idea (different readerships will reconstruct meaning from AI-assisted scholarly output differently) in vocabulary that does not name the present framework's σ and ρ operators but maps onto them structurally. The multiple-discovery pattern within a single multi-author paper is itself an instance of the framework's central claim: independent operators arriving at the same underlying substrate through different rendering choices. The retroactive spine extraction of the Kulkarni et al. paper surfaces eight discrete substrate propositions, of which five are explicitly stated in body sections and three are implicit (inferable from the antecedent-DAG of stated propositions but not numbered as such by the original authors). The retroactive spine's antecedent-DAG matches the paper's actual citation dependency graph at proposition-level granularity, which supplies hypothesis H3 of the validation case.

### *T1 — Historical existence proof (non-management domain; appendix depth).*

The Heisenberg–Schrödinger founding papers of quantum mechanics are the framework's existence proof for P4 in the historical record. Heisenberg (1925) introduced matrix mechanics; Born and Jordan (1925) and Born, Heisenberg, and Jordan (1926) extended it. Schrödinger (1926) introduced wave mechanics through the Quantisierung-als-Eigenwertproblem papers. Schrödinger (1926) himself proved that matrix mechanics and wave mechanics are mathematically equivalent in the equivalence paper *Über das Verhältnis der Heisenberg-Born-Jordanschen Quantenmechanik zu der meinen*; von Neumann (1932) supplied the rigorous Hilbert-space functional-analytic foundation. Pauli (1927), in his magnetic-electron paper, performed both renderings of the spin-electron problem himself within a single paper and stated explicitly that the wave-mechanics treatment was "completely equivalent in its consequences to the matrix equations of Heisenberg and Jordan" — a within-author rendering-equivalence demonstration stronger than the cross-author H–S equivalence because it removes coordination concerns.

*Why retained as existence proof.* The H–S twin pair is the cleanest historical-record demonstration that two renderings of (what was later shown to be) the same underlying spine, in incompatible formalisms, can converge on conclusions. The framework's P4 claim is precisely the structural claim Schrödinger 1926 and von Neumann 1932 demonstrated for that case. Retaining the case at appendix depth (Online Appendix D) supplies historical-record evidence that the rendering-equivalence claim is not a methodological artifact of contemporary management research; the empirical anchor in management research is T1′.

*Why retained at appendix depth.* Domain distance from organizational knowledge work means the quantum-mechanics case engages a reader population disjoint from the framework's primary audience; the cross-disciplinary case supplies the existence proof that the framework's central preservation claim holds across radically different formalisms, while the in-discipline case (T1′) carries the substantive engagement with the management-research audience the framework primarily addresses.

### *T3 — Forward-looking application to an external organizational artifact.*

To demonstrate the spine-first protocol on a non-self artifact, we apply it prospectively to a publicly available organizational methodology handbook. The illustrative target is a published methodology handbook from an organization that produces knowledge-intensive deliverables at volume — for example, a public open-source software-engineering handbook (the GitLab handbook is a frequently-cited candidate; alternatives include published consulting-firm methodology repositories or open-access research-organization knowledge bases). The retroactive spine extraction follows the same protocol the management-theory twin pair extraction follows: the handbook's logic graph is reconstructed at proposition level, antecedent edges typed, evidence anchors mapped to handbook citations, and the resulting substrate published as a companion artifact for downstream verification and recombination.

*Hypotheses.* H1: the extracted substrate surfaces at least one proposition the handbook's prose does not number as such but the antecedent-DAG identifies as central. H2: the extracted substrate's antecedent-DAG aligns with the handbook's cross-reference structure at the chapter level. H3: at least one organizational consumer of the handbook (a user team within or outside the handbook's source organization) is willing to query the substrate directly rather than re-extract from prose.

*Why a forward-looking case.* T3 is the framework applied to an artifact whose original authors did not produce it under the spine-first protocol — a stricter test than the within-paper self-application because cross-author independence holds and the source organization has its own opinion of what the artifact's substrate looks like. Full operational deployment of T3 is the scope of a separate case study (organizational research not in the present paper's page budget); the present paper specifies the protocol the case study applies.

### *T3-prime — Reflexive self-application (robustness check; appendix depth).*

In parallel with T3, the present paper exhibits the framework reflexively by publishing its own substrate as a Zenodo companion artifact and tracing every prose claim back to a substrate entry through the spine-first drafting protocol. A small companion-rendering set extends the reflexive application across language and register: a Russian-language rendering for a non-anglophone audience (operational instance at t.me/spectralbranding); a practitioner article for general professional readers. The reflexive self-application is structurally circular and therefore secondary to T3's external case; it serves as existence-proof evidence that the protocol produces a coherent self-application set. If the protocol could not produce a coherent reflexive application, the framework's operational claim would fail on its strongest case. The reflexive case is treated at appendix depth (Online Appendix D).

## Discussion

### *Primary theoretical contribution.*

The paper makes one primary theoretical contribution: the **Operator** as a role-level abstraction whose intrinsic operations partition into structural-substrate operations and judgment operations, with era-dependent projection composition determining which projection (human or AI) performs each operation class. The contribution is one role architecture, not several extensions: everything else the paper develops — the three-layer decomposition, the propositions P1 and P4, the substrate schema, the spine-first protocol, and the practical Design Propositions below — operationalizes the Operator at progressively more concrete levels of analysis.

The Operator role sits in dialogue with five established literatures without claiming co-equal extension of each. Raisch and Krakowski (2021) frame the automation-augmentation paradox at task level; we move the unit of analysis up one level so the task-level paradox becomes a symptom of an underspecified role architecture rather than a structural contradiction. Murray, Rhymer, and Sirmon (2021) document conjoined-agency forms in organizations; the Operator specifies the role-level architecture inside which conjoined agency is structured. Bechky and Okhuysen (2011) and Bailey and Barley (2010) develop role-coordination theory; the Operator absorbs the AI-projection shift through an internal structural-substrate / judgment partition — operations are intrinsic to the role; projection composition is the era-dependent shell. Star and Griesemer (1989) and Carlile (2004) frame boundary objects across pragmatic boundaries; the spine is a new species of boundary object whose plasticity (fork operations) and robustness (typed-edge catalog) are separately engineered for AI mediation, with negotiation moving into observable substrate-edit operations rather than opaque deliberation. Liebeskind (1996), Nickerson and Zenger (2004), and Felin, Kauffman, and Zenger (2023) develop the knowledge-based theory of the firm; the Operator specifies the role-level architecture inside that apparatus once generative AI alters verification costs. The Lebovitz, Lifshitz-Assaf, and Levina (2022) practitioner finding that professionals engage selectively with AI for critical judgments under opacity, the Leonardi, Pilny, Treem, and Sharma (2024) survey of AI in organizational communication, and the Grodal, Ha, and Hood (2024) account of generative-AI category construction supply convergent observations that the framework formalizes at the role-architecture layer.

### *Where the framework engages but does not extend.*

Three adjacent literatures the framework engages without claiming theoretical extension. *Marchetti and Puranam (2025)* document that less-hierarchical firms exhibit stronger cultures via big-data analysis — speaks to the framework's prediction that substrate-publishing organizations strengthen coordination via shared substrate-as-culture but does not require the present framework's apparatus to be understood. *Seidel-Berente-Baiyere (2025)* and *Seidel and O'Mahony (2014)* develop repertoires of institutionally embedded affordances and concept-coherence-via-representations respectively; the framework's spine is one worked institutionally-embedded affordance repertoire, but the repertoire literature does not require the typed-DAG specification the framework supplies. *Berente, Gu, and Recker (2021)* and *Berente, Seidel, and Safadi (2019)* develop the management-of-AI and data-driven-theory frames in IS; the framework's spine-first protocol parallels the data-driven discipline at the substrate-versus-rendering layer cut without subordinating the present role-theoretic contribution.

### *Design Propositions for the post-AI organization.*

The Operator role generates three formal Design Propositions for organizations operating under boundary conditions C1–C4. Each DP names a structural design choice, the cost-asymmetric mechanism that makes the choice dominant, and a falsifier observable in organizational outcome data.

**DP1 — Substrate as a governed organizational asset.** Organizations operating under C1–C4 should publish the substrate of their knowledge artifacts as a first-class governed asset alongside renderings. *Design choices.* Three: substrate ownership (individual operator versus organization versus journal versus community); audit rights (open inspection versus restricted access versus tiered); credit at parity with rendering authoring versus subordinated to it. *Mechanism.* Published substrate is queryable by downstream agents (both human and AI) at sub-linear cost; unpublished substrate forces re-extraction from rendering at super-linear total cost. Organizations crediting substrate authoring attract Operators whose comparative advantage is the structural-substrate operations, accelerating both verification capacity and recombination throughput. *Falsifier.* Under C1–C4, an organization that publishes substrate-as-governed-asset does not exhibit recombination-rate, verification-cost-efficiency, or operator-attraction advantages over a matched organization that does not, within a 24–36 month observation window.

**DP2 — Role and incentive design partitioned along structural-substrate / judgment lines.** R&D labs, consulting firms, standards bodies, and editorial offices should redesign Operator roles around the structural-substrate / judgment partition rather than around the legacy task-level workflow. *Design choices.* Allocation of AI projection to structural-substrate operations (extraction, DAG maintenance, consistency checking, robustness scans); reservation of human projection for judgment operations (fork-rebase-merge, theoretical commitment, cohort-specific rendering); incentive design that credits judgment-quality independently from substrate-throughput. *Mechanism.* The cost asymmetry $\beta < 1 < \delta$ makes the partition cost-dominated; selective engagement of professionals with AI for critical judgments (Lebovitz, Lifshitz-Assaf, and Levina 2022) becomes structured division of labor rather than idiosyncratic resistance. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) supply empirical anchor at the practitioner-task level: generative AI raises productivity asymmetrically across worker skill levels in ways consistent with the substrate-judgment split (less-experienced workers gain most on the substrate side; most-experienced workers see small gains on speed and small declines on quality). *Falsifier.* Under C1–C4 and post-AI projection composition, organizations adopting the partition do not realize cost or recombination advantages relative to organizations operating under the legacy task-level workflow.

**DP3 — Editorial-process decoupling of substrate-coherence and prose-craft evaluation.** Peer review and editorial workflows in academic publishing should evaluate substrate coherence and prose craft as separate inputs rather than as a single rolled-up review verdict. *Design choices.* Substrate received alongside manuscript as a published companion artifact; substrate-coherence evaluated against structural criteria (well-formed propositions; antecedent edges supported by evidence anchors; rivals surfaced; robustness checks present where central claims warrant them); rendering evaluated against prose-craft criteria; editorial decision retains the two evaluations as separable inputs. *Mechanism.* Decoupling exploits the cost asymmetry at the review layer: substrate-coherence checks are AI-projection-tractable (sub-linear cost); rendering-craft checks remain human-projection (super-linear cost). The decoupling is implementable incrementally within incumbent journal infrastructure. *Falsifier.* Editorial offices that implement the decoupling do not exhibit reduced reviewer-time-to-verdict, higher inter-reviewer reliability on substrate verdicts, or improved manuscript outcome quality (measured by post-publication citation-weighted derivative work) relative to matched offices that retain the rolled-up review.

The three Design Propositions together specify a coherent organizational redesign program. Each DP is incrementally implementable; collectively they restructure the economics of organizational knowledge production along the substrate-judgment partition the Operator role formalizes.

The framework's so-what carries beyond the academic-publishing case the validation cases illustrate. *For post-AI theory construction*, the role-level analysis Grimes et al. (2023) call for is operationalized: theory papers carry both a typed-DAG substrate (machine-tractable; auditable; recombinable) and a prose rendering (cohort-conditional; reconstructable per audience), and the spine-first drafting protocol Online Appendix B specifies makes the substrate a first-class artifact reviewers and downstream operators can inspect. *For peer review processes*, DP3 names the actionable shift: substrate review is AI-projection-tractable at sub-linear cost; prose review remains human-projection at super-linear cost; editorial decisions take the two as separable inputs rather than rolling them into a single verdict the rendering layer dominates. *For organizational knowledge governance*, DP1 names the structural choice: published-substrate organizations capture the recombination throughput the cost asymmetry rewards; rendering-only organizations concentrate verification cost and recombination uncertainty on the human-projection side and cannot route AI projection to the operations where its scaling-curve makes it dominant. The Benbya, Strich, and Tamm (2024) GenAI promises-and-perils taxonomy resolves cleanly under this reading: the promises concentrate on the substrate side; the perils concentrate where AI projection is asked to do judgment work the framework reserves for human projection.

### *Limitations.*

*Single-coder reliability for spine extractions.* The retroactive spine extractions reported here and in the companion paper rely initially on single-coder coding by the author. The companion paper specifies a second-coder pass on a 20% subsample with the κ ≥ .75 threshold on both node typing and edge placement. Below threshold, axiom A1 fails empirically on the subdomain in question and P4 weakens from theorem to probabilistic tendency on that subdomain. The mitigation is on the empirical-companion paper's timetable, not the present paper's.

*Retroactive extraction risk.* The historical-case validation (T1) is necessarily a retroactive reconstruction; the original authors had no concept of a spine artifact. Reviewers may dispute whether what is extracted is "their spine" or "the present author's projection of their spine." The mitigation is the secondary-literature consensus anchor (Schrödinger 1926 equivalence paper; von Neumann 1932) for which propositions are central, and the explicit documentation of coder judgment where invoked. The extracted spines are offered as starting points for debate, not final words.

*Bare-content degradation of P4.* The preservation theorem holds as stated under the strict K-as-typed-DAG definition of conclusions. Under a weaker bare-propositional reading, the ← direction degrades to a high-probability empirical claim. The framework takes the strict definition as central; the bare-content reader will find P4 weaker than a theorem and stronger than a tendency. The boundary is named explicitly.

*Schema complexity as adoption friction.* The substrate schema is intentionally machine-operable. Under the pre-AI projection composition the schema is a usability problem; under the post-AI projection the complexity is the operational form the cost asymmetry predicts. Organizations not yet operating in the post-AI mode will experience the schema as a friction; the framework predicts the friction will diminish as projection composition migrates and provides the migration path the spine-first protocol prescribes.

*Empirical reach of P2 and P3 from this paper alone.* P2 and P3 are theoretically-derived predictions with explicit falsifiers and operational metrics; they are not empirically estimated at organizational outcome scale within the present paper. The empirical estimation is the scope of the companion empirical working paper, whose successive releases target progressively larger cohort N.

### *Future research.*

Three directions are immediate. *First*, inter-coder reliability programs (the κ ≥ .75 threshold on extraction protocol) across multiple twin-pair cases — the empirical-companion paper's iterative cohort growth is one such program, but parallel programs in other disciplines (clinical-trials methodology; legal-precedent analysis; standards-body deliberation) would broaden the empirical reach. *Second*, AI-projection cost-function estimation across model families — the cost functions $c_{AI}(S) = \alpha \cdot |V|^\beta$ and $c_{human}(R) = \gamma \cdot |\text{tokens}|^\delta$ admit empirical fitting once spine corpora exist; the parameter estimates across models would test whether the cost asymmetry is robust to AI-architecture variation. *Third*, governance and incentive design for spine-publication — the cost-asymmetry result is necessary but not sufficient for organizational adoption; the political economy of publishing the substrate (who owns it; how is it credited; how is strategic manipulation of edge typing prevented) is a research program in its own right that the framework does not address but its adoption requires.

## Conclusion

The arrival of capable generative AI inside scholarly knowledge production has surfaced a cost asymmetry across the layers of the artifact: structural substrate becomes cheap to construct, verify, and recombine at AI projection scale; cohort-conditional rendering does not, and may become more costly relative to the new equilibrium because rendering is precisely where human attention remains the comparative advantage. The role-theoretic contribution this paper develops — the Operator with intrinsic structural-substrate and judgment operations, whose human-versus-AI projection composition varies by era — absorbs the shift at the layer of role architecture rather than at the layer of task allocation. Two propositions are primary: meaning and meaningfulness are separately optimizable (P1); rendering-equivalence under spine-preservation is provable as a graph-theoretic preservation theorem under one explicit faithfulness axiom (P4). Two further propositions — recombination advantage for substrate-publishing organizations (P2) and verification-cost asymmetry with $\beta < 1 < \delta$ exponents (P3) — are stated as theoretically-derived predictions with explicit falsifiers and operational metrics, with empirical estimation at organizational outcome scale deferred to a companion working paper. The framework applies under four joint sufficient boundary conditions and explicitly does not apply in five domains where one or more condition is principle-failed or principle-inverted; the explicit negative scope counterbalances the positive scope and addresses the standing concern that role-theoretic framework papers over-claim by ceding five named domains to the prior traditions that already characterize them.

We exhibit the framework reflexively by publishing the present paper's substrate as a Zenodo companion artifact, by tracing every prose claim to a substrate entry through the spine-first drafting protocol, and by producing companion renderings of the same substrate across language and register boundaries. The contemporary management-theory twin pair is the primary illustrative case; the founding papers of quantum mechanics are the historical existence proof at extreme formalism distance; the reflexive self-application is the robustness check. The empirical estimation of the recombination metric and the cost-asymmetry parameters at organizational outcome scale is the scope of a companion empirical working paper. The split between theoretical contribution and empirical estimation preserves a falsification path the framework would not otherwise have: P2 and P3 cannot be tested at the organizational outcome scale a tier-one management journal expects within the page budget of a single theory paper, and the companion path supplies the empirical reach without compressing the theoretical contribution. The reader who accepts P1 and P4 as developed here and wishes to interrogate P2 and P3 empirically has the companion paper as the starting point and a documented trajectory toward large-N falsification.
